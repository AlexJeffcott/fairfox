# Reading Experience

## Movement and Memory

Within a chapter, she can move freely — explore the hub, revisit rooms, look at things again. Between chapters, the killing challenge door seals behind her. She cannot go back.

Past locations appear in the memory palace. Clicking them shows memory-texts — short reflective prose from her current perspective. Not replays. Reconstructions. Memories are coloured by what she knows now: richer, reinterpreted, sometimes imperfect.

## Death

Death resets to the start of the chapter. The reader is the next generation. The bones they pass are their previous attempts.

## Chapter Rhythm

Each chapter contains:

- **Prose**: Walking, observing, surviving. The world between challenges. This is where the reader absorbs — the environment teaches passively before challenges test actively
- **Micro challenges**: Every ~200 words. Low stakes. Teach or revisit a concept. Get one wrong and you miss out on something but keep going. Woven into the prose so they almost don't feel like challenges
- **Litanies**: Found on walls between challenges. Principles of thinking, not solutions to puzzles. The reader accumulates these
- **Killing challenge**: End of chapter. High stakes. Draws on what the micro challenges and previous chapters taught. Get it wrong, you die, you restart the chapter

## Choices

Presented as plain text links after prose. Usually 3-4 options. The wrong answers are plausible — the reader has to actually think, not just pick the one that sounds right.

## Spaced Repetition

Earlier concepts return in later chapters in new forms. The micro challenges handle this quietly — a small moment that tests something from three chapters ago.

## Didactic Approach

The reader learns real concepts — algebra, geometry, logic, physics — by solving problems embedded in the world. Nothing is ever named or explained. The Torus doesn't label its lessons. The reader does the thinking and the story moves on.

Starting level: a bright nine-year-old. Growing with the protagonist as she ages.

## Tone

Prose, not game text. Trust the reader. Imply, don't explain.
