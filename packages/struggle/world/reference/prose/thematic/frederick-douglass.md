# Frederick Douglass

Douglass tells the story of his awakening as a story about reading. His mistress, Mrs Auld, began teaching him the alphabet, until her husband forbade it: *"If you give a nigger an inch, he will take an ell. A nigger should know nothing but to obey his master — to do as he is told to do. Learning would spoil the best nigger in the world."* Douglass heard this and understood it as a revelation — not the one Mr Auld intended: *"From that moment, I understood the pathway from slavery to freedom."* The prohibition was the proof that literacy was power. He learned in secret, trading bread to white boys in the street for lessons, scratching letters on fences and pavements. *"The more I read, the more I was led to abhor and detest my enslavers."* The knowledge was not a comfort. It was a torment. *"I would at times feel that learning to read had been a curse rather than a blessing. It had given me a view of my wretched condition, without the remedy."* Light, in Douglass's telling, brings suffering before it brings freedom — the same structure as Plato's cave, though Douglass lived it.

The physical struggle comes later, in the fight with Covey the slave-breaker. Douglass had been sent to Covey to be broken, and for six months it worked. *"Mr Covey succeeded in breaking me. I was broken in body, soul, and spirit. My natural elasticity was crushed, my intellect languished, the disposition to read departed."* The darkness was complete. Then Covey attacked him one morning and Douglass, for reasons he does not fully explain, fought back. They fought for nearly two hours. Covey could not beat him. Douglass marks this moment with absolute clarity: *"You have seen how a man was made a slave; you shall see how a slave was made a man."* And then: *"It was a glorious resurrection, from the tomb of slavery, to the heaven of freedom. My long-crushed spirit rose, cowardice departed, bold defiance took its place."* He was not yet free — that would take years more. But the darkness had broken. He uses the word *resurrection* deliberately. He had been dead, and was not dead any longer.
