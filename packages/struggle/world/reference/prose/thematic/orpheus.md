# Orpheus

Virgil tells the story at the close of the *Georgics*, and it is among the most beautiful passages he ever wrote. Orpheus descends to the underworld to retrieve Eurydice, and his singing moves even the dead: *"the bloodless ghosts themselves were weeping"* — *ipsae flentes umbrae*. The lords of that kingdom, *"whom no prayer has ever moved,"* relent and grant his wife's return, on one condition: *"ne flectat retro sua lumina"* — he must not turn his eyes back. They walk the silent path upward, Eurydice following behind, and they are almost at the threshold of daylight — *"iamque pedem referens casus evaserat omnes"* — he had already escaped every danger — when, as Virgil puts it with devastating simplicity, *"dementia cepit amantem"*: madness seized the lover. He looked back. *"Ibi omnis effusus labor"* — there all his effort was poured out, wasted. Eurydice slips away, "dying a second time," and her last words carry no reproach, only bewilderment: *"quis et me miseram et te perdidit, Orphee, quis tantus furor?"* — "What madness, Orpheus, what great madness has destroyed both you and me?" (Georgics IV.485–498). Ovid gives the moment a different colour in the *Metamorphoses*: Orpheus turns back *"avidusque videndi"* — greedy to see her — and "she was gone, reaching out her arms, struggling to be held and to hold, and clutching nothing but the yielding air" (*bracchiaque intendens prendique et prendere certans / nil nisi cedentes infelix arripit auras*, Met. X.58–59). The escape was real. It was almost complete. What defeated it was not the darkness but the one who fled it.
