# The Harrowing of Hell

The Western churches tell the resurrection as an empty tomb. The Eastern churches tell it as a prison break. The central icon of Easter in the Orthodox tradition is not the risen Christ in a garden but the *Anastasis* — Christ standing on the shattered gates of Hell, reaching down to pull Adam and Eve out of their tombs by the wrist. The *Gospel of Nicodemus*, which preserves the oldest narrative version, gives the scene in extraordinary detail. Hell speaks as a character — a warden, suddenly afraid. He has heard what this man did to Lazarus: *"He who by a single word drew forth from me one who had been dead four days — what will he do now that he comes himself?"* Satan tells Hell to hold fast. Hell resists: *"If you brought to me the one who pulled Lazarus away as easily as a man draws breath, that man is no dead man — I fear for all my captives."* Then the voice comes from outside: *"Lift up your gates, O ye princes, and be ye lift up, ye everlasting doors, and the King of Glory shall come in."* Hell cries out: *"Who is the King of Glory?"* The doors break. *"And all the dead who were bound were loosed from their chains."* The *Homily on Holy Saturday* attributed to Epiphanius, read in Orthodox churches on the night before Easter, gives Christ's words to Adam: *"Awake, O sleeper, and arise from the dead, and Christ shall give you light. I am your God, who for your sake became your son. Arise, let us go hence; for you in me and I in you, together we are one undivided person."* Christ does not merely open the door. He descends, breaks the architecture of death itself, and walks the dead out by the hand. The emphasis in every Eastern telling is the same: the rescue comes from outside, it is violent, and it leaves the prison in ruins.
