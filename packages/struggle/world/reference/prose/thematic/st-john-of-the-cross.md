# St John of the Cross

John wrote *The Dark Night of the Soul* in the years after his escape from prison — nine months locked in a cell barely six feet wide by the friars of his own order. The poem that anchors the work is deceptively simple: *"En una noche oscura, / con ansias, en amores inflamada, / ¡oh dichosa ventura!, / salí sin ser notada, / estando ya mi casa sosegada"* — "On a dark night, / with longings, in love inflamed, / O happy chance! / I went out unseen, / my house being now at rest." The soul escapes its own house in darkness, and the darkness is not the obstacle. It is the way. John is precise about this: the night has three phases, each darker than the last. The first is the night of the senses, where ordinary pleasures go dark. The second is the night of the spirit, where even the consolations of prayer and devotion are withdrawn. *"God perceives the imperfections within us, and because of his love for us, urges us to grow up… His love is not content to leave us in our weakness, and for this reason he takes us into a dark night."* The soul feels abandoned — and that feeling of abandonment is the work being done. *"The darkness is not darkness to the soul but light; and the night of faith guides it more surely than the light of noon."* This is John's most radical claim: the soul sees better in the dark than in the light, because in the light it sees only itself, and in the dark it must reach for what it cannot see. The poem's last stanza arrives at stillness: *"Quedéme y olvidéme, / el rostro recliné sobre el Amado; / cesó todo y dejéme, / dejando mi cuidado / entre las azucenas olvidado"* — "I stayed, lost to myself, / I laid my face against the Beloved's; / all ceased, and I let go, / leaving my cares / forgotten among the lilies." The struggle is over not because the darkness has lifted but because the soul has stopped needing it to lift.
