# Dante's Purgatorio

The *Inferno* ends in darkness — Dante and Virgil climb down Satan's body at the frozen centre of the earth, pass through a narrow tunnel, and emerge under unfamiliar stars. The first word of the *Purgatorio* is the aftermath of that passage: *"Per correr miglior acque alza le vele / ormai la navicella del mio ingegno"* — "To course over better waters the little vessel of my genius now hoists her sails" (Purg. I.1–2). But the hoisting is the easy part. The mountain of Purgatory must be climbed terrace by terrace, and every step costs effort. On the first steep slope Dante is already gasping, and Virgil tells him plainly how it works: *"Questa montagna è tale, / che sempre al cominciar di sotto è grave; / e quant' om più va sù, e men fa male"* — "This mountain is such that the climb is hardest at the bottom; and the higher one goes, the less it hurts" (Purg. IV.88–90). The pain lessens not because the mountain changes but because the climber does. Each terrace purges a weight — pride, envy, wrath, sloth — and with each weight shed the climbing becomes lighter. The architecture of the canticle is an architecture of effort rewarded. At the summit, Virgil speaks his last words to Dante: *"Non aspettar mio dir più né mio cenno; / libero, dritto e sano è tuo arbitrio"* — "Await no more word or sign from me; your will is free, upright, and whole" (Purg. XXVII.139–140). Then Beatrice appears, and Dante sees her through eyes that have been prepared, step by aching step, to bear the light. The last line of the *Purgatorio* mirrors the last line of the *Inferno* — both end with *stelle*, stars — but the difference is everything. At the end of the *Inferno* he merely sees them again. At the end of the *Purgatorio* he is *"puro e disposto a salire a le stelle"* — "pure and ready to rise to the stars" (Purg. XXXIII.145). The seeing has changed because the seer has.
