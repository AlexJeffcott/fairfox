# Rumi's Masnavi

The *Masnavi* opens with eighteen lines about a reed flute, and they contain everything Rumi will spend six books unfolding. *"Listen to the reed, how it tells a tale, complaining of separations — saying, 'Ever since I was parted from the reedbed, my lament has caused man and woman to moan.'"* The reed has been cut from its source. The sound it makes — the music, the poetry, all of it — is the sound of that separation. *"I want a bosom torn by severance, that I may unfold to such a one the pain of love-desire."* The longing is the point. Rumi does not promise that the darkness lifts easily. The whole of the *Masnavi* circles between stories — the parrot and the merchant, the king and the handmaiden, the lion and the hare — and each story is another way of saying the same thing: you are separated from what you love, and the separation is unbearable, and the unbearableness is what drives you home. *"The wound is the place where the Light enters you."* In the third book, Rumi gives the image most directly: *"The spirit is like a falcon that has been captured and hooded. It sits on the wrist of this world, forgetting the vast sky. But sometimes the hood slips, and for a moment the falcon sees — and the longing to fly becomes greater than the fear of flight."* The struggle in Rumi is never against an enemy. It is against forgetting. The darkness is not a place; it is the hood over the falcon's eyes. And the light is not somewhere else — it is what the falcon sees the instant the hood is lifted. *"What you seek is seeking you."* The work is not to travel a great distance. The work is to stop consenting to the blindfold. But that — as anyone who has tried it knows — is the hardest work there is. The *Masnavi* returns, in its final lines, to where it began: *"This is Love: to fly heavenward, to rend every instant a hundred veils."* Not one veil. A hundred. And not once. Every instant.
