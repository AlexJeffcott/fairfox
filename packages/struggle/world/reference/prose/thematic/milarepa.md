# Milarepa

The *Life of Milarepa*, set down by Tsangnyön Heruka in the fifteenth century from older oral sources, begins in misery. Milarepa's father dies, and his uncle and aunt seize the family's wealth, reducing Milarepa, his mother, and his sister to servants in their own house. His mother, consumed by rage, sends the boy to learn black magic. He learns it. He sends a hailstorm that destroys his uncle's house during a wedding feast, killing thirty-five people. *"The neighbours came rushing to the ruins. When they saw the carnage, they cried out in horror."* His mother is exultant. Milarepa is destroyed by what he has done. He seeks out a teacher and is sent to Marpa the Translator. Marpa accepts him — but will not teach him. Instead, Marpa orders him to build a tower. Milarepa hauls stones up the mountain on his back and builds it. Marpa inspects it, declares it wrong, and orders him to tear it down and carry every stone back to where he found it. *"You must put each stone back in its original place, so that the hillside is as it was before."* Milarepa obeys. Marpa orders him to build again — a different shape, in a different place. He builds it. Marpa orders it torn down. This happens again and again. Milarepa's back becomes a single open sore. He weeps. He despairs. He tries to leave. Marpa's wife, Dakmema, takes pity on him and gives him food, but she cannot intervene. *"Do not despair,"* she tells him. *"Your teacher's actions have a purpose you cannot yet see."* The purpose was this: each tower built and destroyed was a purification. The thirty-five people Milarepa killed required something more than remorse. They required the body's suffering, stone by stone. Only after the towers — after years of labour that seemed meaningless and cruel — did Marpa sit Milarepa down and begin to teach. Milarepa went into the mountains alone, wore nothing but a cotton cloth, ate nothing but nettles until his skin turned green, and meditated in caves for years. The songs he composed there are among the most luminous things in Tibetan literature: *"In the monastery of my body, in the temple of my breast, on the altar of my heart — the lamp of understanding burns bright."* The murderer became the lamp. But the road between was made of stone and pain and obedience to a teacher who seemed to offer nothing but cruelty — and who was, in fact, building the only path that could bear the weight of what Milarepa had done.
