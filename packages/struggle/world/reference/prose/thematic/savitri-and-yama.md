# Savitri and Yama

The *Mahabharata* places this story in the forest, during exile. Savitri chose her husband knowing he would die. The sage Narada warned her: Satyavan was noble, beautiful, generous — and fated to die in exactly one year. *"His span of life is short. In twelve months' time, Satyavan must leave his body."* Her father begged her to choose another. Savitri refused: *"Whether his life be long or whether it be short, once and for all I have chosen my husband. I shall not choose twice."* She married him. She counted the days. On the appointed morning she followed Satyavan into the forest, and when he fell — *"his head racked with pain, weary, longing to sleep"* — she took his head in her lap and held him as Yama appeared. Yama, the god of death, dark and magnificent, *"robed in red, with glowing eyes,"* drew the soul from Satyavan's body with his noose — *"a being the size of a thumb"* — and turned south, toward the land of the dead. Savitri rose and followed. Yama told her to turn back: *"Turn back, Savitri. Perform his funeral rites. You have come as far as it is possible to come."* She did not turn back. She walked with him. Yama, moved by her devotion, offered her boons — any wish, so long as she did not ask for her husband's life. She asked for her father-in-law's eyesight. Granted. She asked for his lost kingdom. Granted. She asked for a hundred sons for her own father. Granted. Then she asked for a hundred sons for herself. Yama granted it — and Savitri answered: *"How shall I have a hundred sons if my husband is dead? I have chosen Satyavan. I ask for no other."* Yama, caught by his own word, released Satyavan's soul. *"So be it,"* he said. *"Here I free him."* The whole exchange is conducted while walking, step by step, further into the realm of death. Savitri carries no weapon, no golden bough, no thread. She carries only the refusal to stop following, and a clarity of mind that turns the god of death's own generosity into the instrument of her husband's return. She does not escape the underworld. She walks into it, calm and precise, and talks it into letting go.
