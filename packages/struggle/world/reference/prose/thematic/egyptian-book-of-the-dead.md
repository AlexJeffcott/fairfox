# The Egyptian Book of the Dead

The Egyptians called it *Pert em Hru* — "the going forth by day" — and the title itself tells you what the text is for. It is not a book about death. It is a book about emerging into light. The soul of the deceased — the *ba* — must navigate the *Duat*, the underworld, through a darkness populated by serpents, demons, lakes of fire, and gates guarded by beings whose names must be spoken correctly or the passage is refused. The *Book of the Dead* is, in essence, a script — the words the dead must say at each threshold to be allowed through. At the first gate: *"I know you, and I know your name, and I know the name of the god who guards you."* At each gate, the same formula, because in Egyptian theology to know the name of a thing is to have power over it. The darkness can be navigated, but only with knowledge. The culmination is the Hall of Ma'at, where the heart of the deceased is placed on one side of a scale and the feather of Ma'at — truth, order, justice — on the other. Anubis watches the balance. Thoth records the result. The monster Ammit — part crocodile, part lion, part hippopotamus — waits beside the scale to devour the heart if it is too heavy. The deceased speaks the Negative Confession: *"I have not done iniquity. I have not robbed with violence. I have not done violence to any man. I have not committed theft. I have not slain man or woman. I have not made light the bushel. I have not acted deceitfully. I have not uttered falsehood."* Forty-two denials, one for each judge. If the heart balances, the soul passes through to the Field of Reeds — *Sekhet-Aaru* — where the grain grows tall, the waterways are full, and the deceased lives in light forever. The most beautiful passage in the entire collection is Spell 15, the hymn to the rising sun, which the soul recites upon emerging: *"Hail to you, Ra, at your rising, at your beauty, at your shining, at your crowning… The Two Lands are bright with your rising. You illumine the Two Lands with your rays. The gods rejoice when they behold you. You cross the sky in peace."* The whole ordeal — the serpents, the gates, the weighing — has been for this: to stand in the sun again. The Egyptians did not call it the Book of the Dead. They called it the Book of Going Forth by Day. The destination was always the light.
