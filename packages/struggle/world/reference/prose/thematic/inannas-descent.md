# Inanna's Descent

The oldest underworld journey we have is Inanna's, and it begins with a line of extraordinary directness: *"From the Great Above she opened her ear to the Great Below."* Inanna, Queen of Heaven, dressed herself in all her powers — the *shugurra*, the crown of the steppe; lapis lazuli beads at her neck; the royal robe around her body — and set out for the land from which no traveller returns. At each of the seven gates of the underworld, the gatekeeper demanded she remove one piece of her regalia. At the first gate, her crown. At the second, her lapis measuring rod. At the third, the beads. At the fourth, the breastplate. At the fifth, the gold ring. At the sixth, the lapis measuring line. At the seventh, her royal robe. *"Naked and bowed low, Inanna entered the throne room."* She stood before her sister Ereshkigal stripped of every power she had carried in with her — and Ereshkigal *"fastened on Inanna the eye of death. She spoke against her the word of wrath. She uttered against her the cry of guilt. She struck her. Inanna was turned into a corpse, a piece of rotting meat, and was hung from a hook on the wall."* Three days she hung there. She was rescued not by her own strength but by the foresight of her servant Ninshubur, who mourned at the gates above and pleaded with the gods until Enki fashioned two small creatures to slip into the underworld and sprinkle Inanna with the food and water of life. Even then, the law of that place held: *"No one ascends from the underworld unmarked."* The demons of Ereshkigal followed her back, demanding a substitute. The return cost her husband, Dumuzi, who was dragged down in her place. Inanna came back from the dead, but she did not come back whole.
