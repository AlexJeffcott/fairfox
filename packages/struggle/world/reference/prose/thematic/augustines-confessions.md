# Augustine's Confessions

Augustine spent years in what he himself called darkness — not ignorance, but a divided will. He knew what was true and could not make himself act on it. The *Confessions* are relentless on this point. *"I was bound not by an iron chain imposed by anyone else, but by the iron of my own will. The enemy held my will, and from it had made a chain and shackled me"* (VIII.v.10). He wanted to want God and could not stop wanting everything else. The famous prayer belongs to this period: *"Da mihi castitatem et continentiam, sed noli modo"* — "Grant me chastity and continence, but not yet" (VIII.vii.17). The garden scene in Milan is the hinge. Augustine is weeping under a fig tree, torn apart by his own irresolution, when he hears a child's voice from a neighbouring house — *"tolle lege, tolle lege"* — "take up and read, take up and read" (VIII.xii.29). He opens Paul's letter to the Romans and his eye falls on the passage: *"Not in rioting and drunkenness, not in chambering and wantonness, not in strife and envying; but put on the Lord Jesus Christ, and make no provision for the flesh."* He reads no further. *"Nec ultra volui legere, nec opus erat"* — "I did not wish to read further, nor was there need" (VIII.xii.29). The darkness breaks. But the breakthrough is not a triumph; it is a surrender. Years later, looking back, Augustine addresses God in the line that holds the whole arc of the book: *"Sero te amavi, pulchritudo tam antiqua et tam nova, sero te amavi!"* — "Late have I loved you, O Beauty ever ancient, ever new, late have I loved you!" And then the image: *"You were within me, and I was outside. You were with me, and I was not with you. You called, you shouted, you broke through my deafness. You flared, you blazed, you drove away my blindness"* (X.xxvii.38). The light was not distant. It had been there all along. What took so long was his willingness to open his eyes.
