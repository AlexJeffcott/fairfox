# Exodus

The Book of Exodus begins in slavery, in the dark of Egypt. The Israelites are making bricks. Pharaoh has ordered every newborn son drowned in the Nile. When God speaks to Moses from the burning bush, the promise is not comfort but motion: *"I have surely seen the affliction of my people which are in Egypt, and have heard their cry by reason of their taskmasters; for I know their sorrows; and I am come down to deliver them"* (3:7–8). Ten plagues follow before Pharaoh relents, and the last — the death of the firstborn — comes in a night so total that the Israelites must mark their doors with blood so that the destroyer will pass them over. They leave in haste, with unleavened bread, and almost immediately the darkness follows them: Pharaoh changes his mind, and his chariots pursue them to the shore of the sea. The crossing is the iconic image — *"And Moses stretched out his hand over the sea; and the LORD caused the sea to go back by a strong east wind all that night, and made the sea dry land, and the waters were divided"* (14:21) — but the crossing is not the end. It is barely the beginning. Forty years of wilderness follow. The people complain. They want to go back. *"Would to God we had died by the hand of the LORD in the land of Egypt, when we sat by the flesh pots, and when we did eat bread to the full"* (16:3). They would rather have slavery with food than freedom with hunger. Moses leads them anyway, through thirst, through rebellion, through apostasy — the golden calf, the broken tablets, the long circling of the same ground. Moses himself never enters the promised land. He sees it from Mount Nebo — *"And the LORD showed him all the land"* (Deut. 34:1) — and dies on the mountain. The escape from darkness is not a single act. It is a generation's work, and the one who began it does not live to see it finished.
