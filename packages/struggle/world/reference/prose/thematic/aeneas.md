# Aeneas

In the sixth book of the *Aeneid*, the Sibyl warns Aeneas what his descent will cost. The famous lines state it plainly: *"facilis descensus Averno; / noctes atque dies patet atri ianua Ditis; / sed revocare gradum superasque evadere ad auras, / hoc opus, hic labor est"* — "The descent to Avernus is easy; the door of dark Dis stands open night and day; but to retrace your steps and escape to the upper air, that is the task, that is the labour" (VI.126–129). Aeneas goes anyway. He carries the golden bough, the token that lets him pass living among the dead. He crosses the Styx, passes through the fields of mourning where he finds Dido — who will not speak to him, turning away "with eyes fixed on the ground, her face no more moved by his pleading than if she were set in hard flint or Marpesian rock" (*quam si dura silex aut stet Marpesia cautes*, VI.471). He walks the deep places. He meets his father Anchises in Elysium, and Anchises shows him the souls of the Romans yet to be born — the whole future of Rome, waiting in the underworld to take on flesh. Then Aeneas returns. Virgil tells us he leaves by the Gate of Ivory, the gate through which false dreams rise to the world above. Scholars have argued for centuries about why Virgil chose that gate. But the passage itself is unambiguous about one thing: Aeneas went down into the place of the dead, saw what no living man should see, and came back. The descent was easy. The return was the work.
