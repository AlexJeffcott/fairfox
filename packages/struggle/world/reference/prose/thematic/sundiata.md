# Sundiata

D.T. Niane's transcription of the griot Djeli Mamoudou Kouyaté's telling begins with a prophecy: a hunter tells the king of Mali that he must marry an ugly, hunchbacked woman, and from her will come the greatest king the world has known. The king marries Sogolon. She gives birth to Sundiata — and the child cannot walk. *"He crawled on all fours and often his mother would weep at the sight."* At the age when other children run and play, Sundiata drags himself through the dirt. The king's first wife, Sassouma Bérété, mocks Sogolon openly: *"I have a son who walks. Your son will crawl forever."* When Sundiata's father dies, Sassouma Bérété seizes power and makes life unbearable for Sogolon and her children. The griot tells us that Sundiata endured it all in silence: *"He had nothing of the great princes about him. Men's eyes were fixed on Dankaran Touman,"* the elder brother, the one who could stand. Then comes the day. Sogolon is weeping because she cannot even get baobab leaves for her sauce — Sassouma Bérété's women have taken the only baobab tree. Sundiata hears his mother weeping. He sends for an iron rod. The griot Balla Fasséké watches. Sundiata grips the rod and pulls himself upright: *"The iron rod was twisted and useless. He threw it away and walked alone. From that moment on, the son of Sogolon was another man."* The rod bends — he is too strong for iron. He walks to the baobab tree, uproots it with his bare hands, and carries it to his mother's door. *"Sogolon, look — here is the baobab tree. From now on it will be yours."* Exile follows — years of wandering from court to court with his mother, scorned and homeless, while the sorcerer-king Soumaoro Kanté conquers Mali. Sundiata grows in exile. He gathers allies. He returns. At the battle of Kirina he defeats Soumaoro and founds the empire that will last centuries. But the moment the griots return to, the image that holds the whole story together, is not the battle. It is the boy on the ground, gripping the iron rod, rising.
