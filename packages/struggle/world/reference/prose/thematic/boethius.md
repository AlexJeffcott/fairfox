# Boethius

Anicius Manlius Severinus Boethius wrote the *Consolation of Philosophy* in a prison cell in Pavia around 524, awaiting execution for treason he almost certainly did not commit. He had been one of the most powerful men in the Roman world — consul, senator, magister officiorum — and now he was alone in the dark, writing. The book opens in self-pity. Boethius is composing tearful verses, attended by the Muses of Poetry, when a woman appears: tall, with burning eyes, dressed in a robe she has woven herself, torn at the hem by the hands of lesser philosophers who have tried to carry off pieces of her. It is Philosophy. She looks at the weeping Muses and dismisses them in a line of magnificent contempt: *"Who has allowed these theatrical tarts to approach this sick man's bedside? They have no medicine for his sorrows — only sweetened poisons."* She sits down. She tells Boethius that he has forgotten who he is. *"You have forgotten your own nature"* — and the entire book is the process of remembering.

Philosophy leads him through dialogue, from his immediate complaint (the world is unjust, the wicked prosper, the good suffer) toward something harder and more permanent. On fortune: *"What is it about human misery that makes you cling to it so? Even your happiness, when you had it, was never free from anxiety."* On the turning of the wheel: *"If you are trying to stop her wheel from turning, you are the most foolish of men. If it were to stop, it would cease to be Fortune."* The wheel cannot be stopped at the top. Expecting it to stop is the source of suffering, not the turning itself. Gradually, through five books of alternating prose and verse, Philosophy brings Boethius to a higher vantage. The crucial turn comes when she addresses the question of providence and fate. All things that happen under fate are ordered by providence — *"the unfolding of all things in time is, when seen in the mind of God, simply Providence; but when that same unfolding is seen in relation to the things it moves and arranges, it has from ancient times been called Fate."* The prisoner cannot change his circumstances. He can change what he sees. The last poem in the book is a prayer: *"Grant, Father, that my mind may rise to your august abode, grant me to visit the fountain of the good… You are the serenity, the tranquil peace of the virtuous. To see you is the end."* Boethius was executed shortly after finishing the book. The light he found did not save his life. It saved everything else.
