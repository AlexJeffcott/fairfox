# Plato's Cave

In the seventh book of the *Republic*, Socrates asks Glaucon to imagine prisoners chained since childhood in an underground den, their legs and necks fettered so that they can only see the wall before them. Behind them a fire burns, and between fire and prisoners a low wall runs, along which people carry figures and vessels — so that the prisoners see only shadows, and take these shadows for the whole of reality. Then one prisoner is freed. He is "compelled to stand up and turn his neck round and walk and look towards the light," and the light of the fire "will distress him, and he will be unable to see the realities of which in his former state he had seen the shadows" (515c). He is dragged up "the steep and rugged ascent" into the sunlight, and at first he is blinded — "he will not be able to see anything at all of what are now called realities." His eyes must adjust gradually: first shadows, then reflections in water, then things themselves, and at last the sun, which he comes to understand as "the cause of all things" (516b–c). The escape is not a liberation that happens to him. It is painful, and at every stage he wants to turn back to what was familiar. Plato is precise about this: the man "would rather suffer anything than entertain these false notions and live in this miserable manner" only after the adjustment is complete. Before that, he resists. The ascent out of darkness is not a gift. It is work that must be done against every instinct.
