# Attar's Conference of the Birds

The *Mantiq al-Tayr* begins with a gathering. The birds of the world convene and learn that they have a king — the Simorgh — who dwells beyond seven valleys. The hoopoe, who has been there, will guide them. Most of the poem is taken up with excuses. The nightingale cannot leave because of its love for the rose. The hawk is content at the king's wrist. The heron cannot leave the marshes, the owl its ruins. Each bird has a reason not to go, and the hoopoe answers each in turn. Thousands set out. The seven valleys are the Valley of the Quest, the Valley of Love, the Valley of Knowledge, the Valley of Detachment, the Valley of Unity, the Valley of Bewilderment, and the Valley of Poverty and Annihilation. Attar gives each its character. In the Valley of Love: *"The span of this valley is beyond the range of words; here even fire is a frozen thing. When the fire of love blazes up, it burns even the harvest of reason."* In the Valley of Bewilderment: *"Here the heart is plunged into confusion, and sorrow and anguish are born. Here is sighing without relief, a burning pain no remedy can ease — a longing that can neither be endured nor cast aside."* Birds fall along the way — by the hundreds, by the thousands. Some die of thirst, some of exhaustion, some simply turn back. Of the original multitude, thirty birds reach the end. They arrive at the court of the Simorgh, ragged and diminished, and ask to be admitted. A chamberlain turns them away: *"What are you who come here? Go back, you wretched things."* They will not go back. *"We have come so far, we have nothing left; if we are turned away, we shall die at this door."* They are admitted, and what they find is the poem's great turn. *Si murgh* in Persian means "thirty birds." They look into the face of the Simorgh and see themselves. *"In the reflection of each other's faces, these thirty birds of the outer world contemplated the face of the Simorgh of the inner world… When they gazed at the Simorgh, they perceived that it was indeed the Simorgh that was those thirty birds, and when they turned their gaze upon themselves, they saw that they themselves were the Simorgh."* The journey did not take them to something else. It stripped away everything that was not them, until only the essential remained.
