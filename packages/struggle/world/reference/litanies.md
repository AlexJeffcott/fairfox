# Litanies

Found on walls throughout the Torus. The accumulated wisdom and errors of predecessors. Not a curriculum — a living, contradictory, sometimes wrong record of people trying to think clearly under pressure.

She must evaluate each one. The reader learns discernment.

---

## Core Litanies

- **Gendlin**: *What is true is already so. Saying it doesn't make it worse. Not saying it doesn't make it go away. And if it is true, I want to know.*
- **Hold on tightly, let go lightly**: Commit fully to your best understanding. Drop it the instant the evidence changes.
- **Tarski**: *If X, I desire to believe X. If not-X, I desire to believe not-X. Let me not become attached to beliefs I may not want.*
- **The Litany Against Fear** (adapted): *I must not fear. Fear is the mind-killer. I will face my fear. I will let it pass through me.*
- **The Meditation on Curiosity**: *If the box contains a diamond, I desire to believe that the box contains a diamond. If the box does not contain a diamond, I desire to believe that the box does not contain a diamond.*
- **The Map and the Territory**: Your model is not the world. When they disagree, the world is right.
- **Ockham's Razor**: The simplest explanation that fits is preferred — not because it's always right, but because complexity needs justification.
- **Feynman**: *The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool.*

## On Updating Beliefs

- **Conservation of expected evidence**: If you expect the evidence to confirm your belief, you should already believe it more. Evidence you can predict isn't evidence.
- **Strength of evidence**: How much a piece of evidence should change your mind depends on how unlikely it would be if you were wrong.
- **Prior probability**: What you believed before the evidence matters. Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence.
- **Sunk cost**: What you've already spent doesn't affect what you should do now. The bones behind you don't make the path ahead more likely to be safe.

## On Thinking Clearly

- **Confirmation bias**: You notice evidence that supports what you already believe. Fight this.
- **The planning fallacy**: Things take longer and cost more than you expect. Always.
- **Motivated reasoning**: You find reasons for what you want to be true. The Torus doesn't care what you want.
- **The Dunning-Kruger frontier**: You don't know what you don't know. The most dangerous moment is when you feel certain.
- **Survivorship bias**: You only see the people who made it this far. You don't see the ones who tried this path and died — unless the bones tell you.

## From Stoic Tradition

- **Dichotomy of control**: Some things are in your power and some are not. Focus only on what is in your power.
- **Memento mori**: You will die. (In the Torus, this isn't philosophy. It's fact.)
- **Premeditatio malorum**: Imagine the worst that could happen. Then prepare for it.
- **Amor fati**: Love your fate. Not because it's good, but because it's yours.

## From Scientific Tradition

- **Nullius in verba**: Take nobody's word for it.
- **Strong opinions, weakly held**: Commit to your best guess. Abandon it completely when the evidence changes.
- **The principle of charity**: Assume the most reasonable interpretation. But verify.
- **Reproducibility**: If you can't do it again, you didn't understand it the first time.

## Unreliable Litanies

Found throughout — some true, some not, some dangerous.

- *The obvious answer is a trap.* (Sometimes. Sometimes the obvious answer is just the answer.)
- *Trust the pattern.* (Good advice until the pattern changes.)
- *Go back. There is nothing ahead but death.* (Written by someone who stopped. Wisdom or fear?)
- *The warm passage is safe.* (Was it? For them?)
- *If you can't see the whole system, you don't understand any of it.* (Paralysing if taken literally.)
- *Never stop.* (Good advice or someone's desperation?)
- *Nullius in verba.* (Including the words on these walls.)
