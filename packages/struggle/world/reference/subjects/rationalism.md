# Rationalism

## What it is

Rationalism is the commitment to using reason — systematically, honestly, and ruthlessly — to build accurate models of reality and make better decisions. It is both an ancient philosophical tradition (knowledge through reason rather than sense experience alone) and a modern movement (the art of overcoming cognitive biases to actually think clearly).


## Core concepts

### The map and the territory
Alfred Korzybski's dictum, central to modern rationalism. Your beliefs are a map. Reality is the territory. The map is not the territory. When the map and territory disagree, the territory wins. The goal of rationalism is to make the map match the territory as closely as possible — and to remember that it never matches perfectly.

### Epistemic rationality vs instrumental rationality
Epistemic rationality is the art of having true beliefs — of making your map match the territory. Instrumental rationality is the art of achieving your goals — of navigating the territory effectively using your map. They are related but not identical. You can have true beliefs and still make bad decisions. You can sometimes achieve goals through lucky false beliefs. But in the long run, accurate maps serve you better.

### Cognitive biases as systematic errors
Kahneman and Tversky's research programme, adopted as foundational by the rationalist community. Humans do not make random errors in reasoning — they make systematic, predictable errors. Anchoring, availability, representativeness, confirmation bias, the planning fallacy, scope insensitivity, the affect heuristic. These are not character flaws. They are features of human cognition that evolved for ancestral environments and misfire in modern ones. Knowing the catalogue of biases is necessary but not sufficient — you must also develop the habit of checking for them.

### Confirmation bias
The tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms existing beliefs, and to ignore or discount information that contradicts them. The most pervasive bias. It is not merely that people are stubborn — the human brain literally processes confirming and disconfirming evidence differently. Overcoming confirmation bias requires actively seeking out information that could prove you wrong.

### The sequences
Eliezer Yudkowsky's extended blog-post series (2006-2009, later compiled as Rationality: From AI to Zombies) that became the foundational text of the modern rationalist community. Covers: what evidence is, how to update beliefs, why minds fail, quantum mechanics as a case study in reasoning under confusion, and the meta-level question of what it means to try to be rational. Dense, occasionally brilliant, occasionally wrong, always provocative.

### System 1 and System 2
Kahneman's framework (from Stanovich and West). System 1 is fast, automatic, intuitive — it generates impressions and feelings without effort. System 2 is slow, deliberate, effortful — it does calculations and weighs evidence. Most of the time, System 1 runs the show. Rationality, in practice, is the art of knowing when to engage System 2 and training System 1 to hand off more often.

### The outside view
Kahneman and Lovallo's distinction. The inside view: you look at a specific situation, consider its unique features, and make a prediction. The outside view: you look at a reference class of similar situations and base your prediction on how those turned out. The inside view feels more informative. The outside view is almost always more accurate. The planning fallacy occurs because people use the inside view for time estimates. Reference class forecasting uses the outside view.

### Reference class forecasting
A specific technique: when predicting an outcome, find a class of similar past situations and use their distribution of outcomes as your base prediction. Adjust from there if you have specific evidence. Developed by Kahneman and Tversky, formalised by Bent Flyvbjerg. Shown to dramatically improve prediction accuracy for large projects.

### Aumann's agreement theorem
If two rational agents with common priors share their posterior probabilities, they cannot agree to disagree. The theorem assumes common knowledge of each other's posteriors — which never holds in practice — but it provides a useful ideal. Persistent disagreement between informed people suggests at least one of them is not updating correctly, or they have different priors, or they are not sharing information honestly.

### Motivated reasoning
Reasoning that is driven by what you want to conclude rather than by the evidence. A specific form of bias where your desires corrupt your inferences. The soldier mindset (Julia Galef): treating reasoning as a battle to win rather than a search for truth. The opposite is the scout mindset: treating reasoning as an exploration, where being surprised is valuable information.

### The scout mindset
Julia Galef's framework. The scout's job is to map the territory accurately, regardless of what the map reveals. The soldier's job is to defend a position. Most people default to soldier mindset in arguments, relationships, and self-image. Scouts actively seek out evidence that might prove them wrong, because accurate maps are more valuable than comfortable ones.

### Chesterton's fence
Before removing a fence (or changing a rule, or disrupting a system), understand why it was put there. If you do not understand why it exists, you do not understand the consequences of removing it. This is a rationalist principle of humility: systems are often more complex than they appear, and "obvious" improvements frequently have hidden costs.

### The illusion of transparency
People overestimate how well others understand their mental states, and overestimate how well they understand others'. You think your intentions are obvious. They are not. This leads to miscommunication, frustration, and conflict — all of which compound in high-stress environments.

### Goodhart's law
"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Any proxy metric will be gamed once people optimise for it. Standardised test scores, GDP, citation counts, body count in warfare. The map (the metric) gets optimised at the expense of the territory (the thing the metric was supposed to track).

### Making beliefs pay rent
A core rationalist principle: beliefs must have observable consequences. If your belief about the world makes no predictions that could distinguish it from the alternative, it is not a belief about the world — it is a label, a feeling, or a confusion. "What would change your mind?" is the rationalist's challenge. If nothing would, you are not holding a belief — you are holding an identity.

### The reversal test
Nick Bostrom and Toby Ord's technique for detecting status quo bias. If you oppose increasing X, would you also oppose decreasing X? If you would oppose both changes, you are probably biased toward the current value of X rather than reasoning about the optimal value.

### Sunk cost fallacy
The tendency to continue investing in something because of what has already been invested, rather than based on future expected value. "We've come this far" is not a reason to continue if the path ahead is worse than turning back.

### Updating and evidence
The core practice: encountering new information and changing your beliefs proportionally to the strength of the evidence. Strong evidence should cause large updates. Weak evidence should cause small updates. No evidence should cause no update. The amount of update should be proportional to the likelihood ratio — how much more likely the evidence is under one hypothesis than another.

### Litany of Tarski
"If the box contains a diamond, I desire to believe that the box contains a diamond. If the box does not contain a diamond, I desire to believe that the box does not contain a diamond." The rationalist's prayer. The commitment to wanting true beliefs regardless of their emotional valence.

### Litany of Gendlin
"What is true is already so. Owning up to it doesn't make it worse. Not being open about it doesn't make it go away. And because it's true, it is what is there to be interacted with." The companion to the Litany of Tarski — not just accepting truth, but recognising that reality does not change based on your willingness to acknowledge it.


## Key thinkers

**Rene Descartes** (1596-1650) — French philosopher who founded modern rationalism. "I think, therefore I am" — the attempt to rebuild knowledge from the ground up using reason alone. His method of systematic doubt remains the template for rationalist inquiry.

**Baruch Spinoza** (1632-1677) — Dutch philosopher who pushed rationalism further than Descartes. Attempted to derive an entire metaphysics and ethics from axioms using geometric method. Demonstrated both the power and the limits of pure reason applied to reality.

**Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz** (1646-1716) — German polymath who co-invented calculus and envisioned a universal language of reasoning (characteristica universalis) that would settle all disputes through calculation. "Let us calculate!" — the dream of reducing all disagreements to formal logic.

**Eliezer Yudkowsky** (b. 1979) — American AI researcher and writer. Founded the modern rationalist community through his Sequences on LessWrong. Developed the framework of applied rationality that treats cognitive biases as engineering problems to be solved. Also known for work on AI alignment, which he frames as the ultimate rationality challenge.

**Daniel Kahneman** (1934-2024) — Israeli-American psychologist. Won the Nobel Prize in Economics for work (with Tversky) on systematic biases in human judgment. His work provides the empirical foundation — the catalogue of bugs — that the rationalist movement attempts to patch.

**Keith Stanovich** (b. 1950) — Canadian psychologist who coined the terms "System 1" and "System 2" (later popularised by Kahneman) and developed the concept of dysrationalia — the failure to think rationally despite adequate intelligence. Distinguished between intelligence and rationality as separate cognitive abilities.

**Philip Tetlock** (b. 1954) — American political scientist who studied expert prediction for decades. Found that most experts predict no better than chance — but that a small subset ("superforecasters") consistently outperform by using rationalist techniques: Bayesian updating, reference class forecasting, seeking disconfirming evidence, calibrating confidence. His work demonstrates that good forecasting is a learnable skill.

**Julia Galef** (b. 1983) — American writer and podcaster who developed the scout mindset framework and co-founded the Center for Applied Rationality. Her work translates rationalist principles into accessible practice.

**Scott Alexander** (b. 1984) — American psychiatrist and blogger (Slate Star Codex, Astral Codex Ten). The most influential writer in the second wave of the rationalist community. Known for rigorous analysis of social science, medicine, and policy. Demonstrates rationalist thinking applied to real-world questions.


## Connections

**Probability** — Probability is the mathematical language of rationalism. Bayesian updating, calibration, expected value — these are the quantitative tools that make rationalist thinking precise rather than vague. Rationalism without probability is philosophy; with probability, it becomes engineering.

**Empiricism** — The rationalist-empiricist debate is ancient, but in practice they are complementary. Rationalism provides the framework for reasoning; empiricism provides the data. The modern rationalist community is deeply empiricist in practice — it just insists on interpreting evidence correctly.

**Epistemology** — Rationalism is a form of applied epistemology. It takes epistemological questions (what counts as evidence? how should beliefs be justified?) and turns them into practical methods. The rationalist community's contribution is making epistemology operational rather than purely theoretical.

**Logic** — Logic provides the deductive backbone of rationalism. Valid inference, detection of fallacies, the structure of arguments — these are the formal tools. Rationalism extends logic into the probabilistic domain, but logic remains the skeleton.

**Stoicism** — Rationalism and Stoicism share the commitment to governing action by reason rather than emotion. The Stoic discipline of assent — withholding judgment until you have examined an impression — is a proto-rationalist practice. The Stoic emphasis on distinguishing what you can control from what you cannot parallels the rationalist emphasis on distinguishing signal from noise.

**Ethics** — Rationalism applied to ethics produces effective altruism, utilitarian calculation, and the attempt to do the most good per unit of resources. The rationalist community is deeply entangled with ethical questions about AI risk, existential threats, and long-term consequences.

**Decision-making** — Rationalism is the theory; decision-making is the practice. Every rationalist concept — updating, expected value, reference class forecasting, avoiding bias — feeds into the art of making good decisions under uncertainty.

**Emotional intelligence** — The rationalist community initially neglected emotional intelligence, treating emotions as noise to be suppressed. The more mature view (Galef, CFAR) recognises that emotional awareness is necessary for rationality — you cannot correct for biases you do not notice, and many biases are emotional in origin.

**Algebra** — Formal logic, which underpins rationalist reasoning, shares deep structural connections with algebra. Boolean algebra is both logical and algebraic. The rationalist dream of "let us calculate" literally involves algebraic manipulation of beliefs.
