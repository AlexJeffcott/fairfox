# Algebra

## What it is

Algebra is the language of structure and relationship. It is the discovery that
you can reason about unknown quantities by expressing their relationships to
known quantities, and that the relationships themselves can be studied,
manipulated, and generalised. At its deepest, algebra is about the architecture
of mathematical systems — what remains invariant when everything else changes.


## Core concepts

### Variables as unknowns

The foundational move: using a symbol to stand for something you don't know yet.
This seems trivial — it is not. Before al-Khwarizmi, mathematical problems
were stated and solved in words. The introduction of a placeholder for the
unknown ("the thing," al-shay, which became x through Arabic-to-Spanish
translation) transformed mathematics from a collection of specific solutions
into a general method for solving entire classes of problems at once.

### Equivalence and substitution

Two expressions are equivalent if they have the same value for all valid inputs.
If a = b, you can replace a with b anywhere without changing the truth of a
statement. This principle — substitution — is the engine of algebraic
manipulation. It is also a profound idea: the claim that two things that look
different are, in the ways that matter, the same.

### Equations as balance

An equation is a statement that two expressions are equal — a scale in
equilibrium. Whatever you do to one side, you must do to the other. This
physical metaphor (balance, weight, equality) grounds abstract manipulation in
something intuitive: the preservation of a relationship through
transformation. Solving an equation means performing operations that isolate
the unknown while maintaining balance.

### Functions as machines

A function takes an input and produces an output according to a rule. f(x) = 2x + 3
is a machine that doubles and adds three. This is a more powerful idea than it
appears. Functions reframe algebra from "find the unknown" to "understand the
relationship between quantities." The unknown is not a fixed number to be
found but a variable that can take many values, and the function describes how
outputs depend on inputs.

### Linear relationships

The simplest non-trivial relationship: y = mx + b. A constant rate of change,
a straight line. Linear relationships are everywhere — in physics
(velocity under constant acceleration), economics (supply curves), engineering
(proportional control). Understanding linearity is foundational because it is
the base case: many complex systems are studied by approximating them as
locally linear (linearisation, Taylor series, etc.).

### Systems of equations

Multiple relationships constraining multiple unknowns simultaneously. A single
equation in two unknowns has infinitely many solutions; two equations in two
unknowns (if consistent and independent) have exactly one. Systems of equations
model situations where multiple constraints apply at once — which is to say,
nearly all real situations. Methods for solving them (substitution, elimination,
matrix methods) are the workhorse tools of applied mathematics.

### Proportional reasoning

If a is to b as c is to d, then a/b = c/d. Proportional reasoning — the
ability to think in terms of ratios and scaling — is the bridge between
arithmetic and algebra. It underlies percentages, rates, similarity,
dimensional analysis, and much of everyday quantitative reasoning. People who
think proportionally see structure that others miss: "this is growing at the
same rate as that" is a proportional insight.

### Inverse relationships

When one quantity increases, the other decreases (and their product remains
constant). Pressure and volume. Speed and travel time. Concentration and
dilution. Inverse relationships are as fundamental as linear ones but less
intuitive — human perception tends to favour additive relationships over
multiplicative ones. Recognising inverse proportionality is a key algebraic
skill.

### Exponential growth

A quantity that grows by a constant percentage per unit time: f(t) = a * b^t.
Exponential growth is the most underestimated pattern in human cognition.
Populations, compound interest, viral spread, nuclear chain reactions — all
exponential. The human mind, adapted for linear estimation, consistently
underestimates exponential growth. Understanding exponential relationships is
not just mathematical literacy — it is survival-relevant in any environment
where feedback loops operate.

### Logarithms

The inverse of exponentiation. If b^x = y, then log_b(y) = x. Logarithms
compress exponential scales into linear ones, making the enormous manageable.
They are essential for understanding orders of magnitude, measuring sound
(decibels), earthquakes (Richter scale), and information (bits). More deeply,
logarithms represent a fundamental symmetry: addition on a logarithmic scale
corresponds to multiplication on a linear scale.

### Recursion

A process defined in terms of itself. The Fibonacci sequence: each term is the
sum of the two before it. A recursive function calls itself with a smaller
input. Recursion is how complex structures emerge from simple rules applied
repeatedly. It appears in mathematics, computer science, linguistics, biology,
and art. Understanding recursion means understanding self-reference, base cases,
and the way local rules generate global patterns.

### Symmetry groups

A symmetry is a transformation that leaves something unchanged. Rotating a
square 90 degrees maps it onto itself. The set of all symmetries of an object,
with composition as the operation, forms a group. This is where algebra
becomes architecture: symmetry groups classify the fundamental structures of
geometry, physics, chemistry, and art. The group concept — a set with an
operation that satisfies closure, associativity, identity, and inverse — is
one of the most powerful abstractions in mathematics.

### Abstract algebra: groups, rings, fields

The study of algebraic structures in their most general form.

- **Groups:** A set with a single operation satisfying four axioms (closure,
  associativity, identity, inverse). Groups capture symmetry. The integers under
  addition, the rotations of a polygon, the permutations of a set — all groups.

- **Rings:** A set with two operations (addition and multiplication) where
  addition forms a commutative group and multiplication is associative and
  distributes over addition. The integers are a ring. Polynomial rings
  generalise the algebra you learned in school.

- **Fields:** A ring where multiplication also forms a commutative group
  (every non-zero element has a multiplicative inverse). The rationals, reals,
  and complex numbers are fields. Fields are where you can do everything:
  add, subtract, multiply, divide.

These structures are not abstract for the sake of abstraction. They reveal
that apparently different mathematical systems (clock arithmetic, polynomial
manipulation, matrix operations, geometric transformations) share the same
deep structure — and that theorems proved about the structure apply to all
instances.

### The power of notation

Mathematical notation is compressed thought. The equals sign (=), variables (x),
exponents (x^n), function notation (f(x)), sigma notation for sums — each of
these was an invention that made new thoughts possible. Before good notation,
algebra was done in prose (rhetorical algebra), and problems that take a modern
student five lines took pages of text. Notation is not just shorthand — it is
cognitive technology that shapes what can be conceived and communicated.

### Algebra as compressed thought

The deepest way to understand algebra: it is the practice of compressing
complex relationships into manipulable form. An equation is a compressed story
about how quantities relate. Solving it is unpacking that story. Generalising
it is recognising that many stories share the same structure. Abstract algebra
is the study of the structures themselves, independent of any particular story.
This compression is what gives algebra its extraordinary power — and its
frequent inaccessibility, because compressed thought requires decompression
to be understood.

### Polynomials

Expressions built from variables and constants using addition, subtraction,
multiplication, and non-negative integer exponents. Polynomials are the most
studied objects in algebra — they approximate other functions (Taylor series),
encode data (interpolation), and their roots reveal deep structure (the
fundamental theorem of algebra guarantees every polynomial has roots in the
complex numbers). The problem of finding polynomial roots drove centuries of
algebraic development, from the quadratic formula through Galois theory.

### The fundamental theorem of algebra

Every non-constant polynomial with complex coefficients has at least one
complex root. Equivalently: every polynomial of degree n has exactly n roots
(counted with multiplicity) in the complex numbers. This theorem unifies
algebra and analysis, guarantees the completeness of the complex number system,
and provides the foundation for polynomial factorisation.

### Matrices and linear algebra

Matrices are rectangular arrays of numbers that represent linear
transformations — operations that preserve addition and scalar multiplication.
Matrix algebra (addition, multiplication, inversion, diagonalisation) is the
computational backbone of modern science and engineering. Systems of linear
equations, geometric transformations, quantum states, network flows, machine
learning — all are expressed and solved through linear algebra.


## Key thinkers

**al-Khwarizmi (c. 780-850)** — Wrote Al-Kitab al-Mukhtasar fi Hisab al-Jabr
wal-Muqabala (The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing),
from which the word "algebra" derives. Systematised the solution of linear and
quadratic equations. His name, Latinised as "Algoritmi," gives us "algorithm."
He did not invent algebra from nothing — Babylonian and Indian mathematics
preceded him — but he formalised it as a distinct discipline.

**Diophantus of Alexandria (c. 200-284)** — Often called the "father of algebra"
for his Arithmetica, which introduced symbolic abbreviations for unknowns and
operations. Studied equations requiring integer solutions (Diophantine equations).
His work preserved Greek mathematical tradition and influenced Arabic and
European mathematics.

**Francois Viete (1540-1603)** — Introduced systematic symbolic notation, using
letters for both unknowns and known quantities. Before Viete, algebra could
express "the unknown" but not "any number." His notation made general statements
possible and is the direct ancestor of modern algebraic notation.

**Rene Descartes (1596-1650)** — Unified algebra and geometry by inventing
coordinate systems (Cartesian coordinates). This allowed geometric problems to
be translated into algebraic equations and vice versa. Also established many
notational conventions still in use (using x, y, z for unknowns and a, b, c
for constants).

**Leonhard Euler (1707-1783)** — The most prolific mathematician in history.
Standardised mathematical notation (e, i, pi, sigma, f(x)), made foundational
contributions to every branch of mathematics, and established the function
concept as central to algebra and analysis. His identity e^(i*pi) + 1 = 0
connects algebra's five most important constants.

**Evariste Galois (1811-1832)** — Died in a duel at age 20, but not before
inventing group theory and proving that there is no general formula for
solving polynomial equations of degree five or higher. Galois theory connects
the solvability of equations to the symmetry structure of their roots — a
connection that was centuries ahead of its time and remains central to modern
algebra.

**Emmy Noether (1882-1935)** — Transformed abstract algebra and mathematical
physics. Her work on ring theory and ideal theory restructured algebra. Noether's
theorem — that every continuous symmetry corresponds to a conservation law —
is one of the deepest results connecting algebra and physics. Einstein called
her the most important woman in the history of mathematics.

**Alexander Grothendieck (1928-2014)** — Rebuilt algebraic geometry from the ground
up using category theory and schemes, creating frameworks of extreme
abstraction and generality. His approach — finding the right level of
abstraction at which problems become simple — exemplifies algebraic thinking
at its most powerful and most demanding.


## Connections

### Probability
Probability theory is built on algebraic foundations — probability spaces,
sigma-algebras, random variables as measurable functions. Expected value is an
algebraic operation. Combinatorics (counting arrangements and selections) is
the algebraic engine of discrete probability. Bayesian updating is algebraic
manipulation of conditional probabilities.

### Rationalism
Algebra is the exemplar of rationalist knowledge — truths derived from axioms
through pure reasoning, independent of sensory experience. Descartes (himself a
rationalist philosopher) unified algebra and geometry. The axiomatic method in
abstract algebra (define axioms, derive theorems) is rationalism in its purest
mathematical form.

### Empiricism
The tension between algebra and empiricism is productive. Algebra provides
theoretical structure; empirical observation provides data. Function discovery
in the Torus is the empirical side of algebra — inferring relationships from
observations. Curve fitting, regression, and statistical modelling are
algebraic tools applied to empirical data.

### Epistemology
Algebra raises epistemological questions: what do we know when we know that
x = 3? Is mathematical knowledge discovered or invented? Are algebraic
structures real features of the universe or human constructions? Godel's
incompleteness theorems show that any sufficiently powerful algebraic system
contains truths it cannot prove — a fundamental epistemological limit.

### Logic
Algebra and logic are deeply intertwined. Boolean algebra formalises logic
itself. Algebraic logic (Boole, De Morgan) showed that logical reasoning can
be treated as algebraic manipulation. The distinction between syntax (symbolic
manipulation) and semantics (meaning) runs through both fields.

### Stoicism
The connection runs through the discipline of assent and the practice of
distinguishing what can be changed from what cannot. Algebraic thinking
partitions problems into knowns and unknowns, controllables and constraints —
a structural parallel to the Stoic dichotomy of control. Both disciplines
demand that you work within limits rather than rage against them.

### Ethics
Utilitarian calculus is algebraic — maximise the sum of utilities subject to
constraints. Game theory (Nash equilibria, Pareto optimality) uses algebraic
structures to model ethical and strategic interaction. Social choice theory
(Arrow's impossibility theorem) proves algebraically that certain intuitively
reasonable fairness criteria cannot all be simultaneously satisfied.

### Decision-making
Expected value and expected utility are algebraic computations. Decision
matrices formalise trade-offs. Linear programming optimises decisions under
constraints. Game theory models strategic decisions algebraically. The
algebraic formalisation of decisions enables analysis — and also reveals
the limits of formalisation when human judgement, values, and uncertainty
resist quantification.

### Emotional intelligence
The connection is through cognitive capacity and the aesthetics of
understanding. Mathematical anxiety is a specific emotional phenomenon that
degrades algebraic performance. Conversely, the experience of algebraic
insight — seeing a pattern click into place, finding an elegant solution —
is an emotional experience that motivates mathematical work.
