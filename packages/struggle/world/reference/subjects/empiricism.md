# Empiricism

## What it is

Empiricism is the position that knowledge comes from observation — from looking at the world and letting it tell you what is true, rather than deducing truths from first principles. It is the philosophical foundation of the scientific method and the engine of every advance in understanding that depends on evidence rather than authority.


## Core concepts

### The scientific method
Not a single method but a family of practices: observe, hypothesise, predict, test, revise. The core insight is that theories must make predictions that could be wrong, and those predictions must be checked against reality. When theory and reality disagree, theory loses. This sounds obvious. It took humanity thousands of years to institutionalise it.

### Falsifiability
Karl Popper's demarcation criterion. A theory is scientific if and only if it makes predictions that could, in principle, be shown to be wrong. "All swans are white" is falsifiable — find one black swan and it is refuted. "The universe was created by an unfalsifiable being" is not scientific — not because it is false, but because no observation could distinguish it from the alternative. Falsifiability does not tell you what is true. It tells you what is capable of being tested.

### The problem of induction
David Hume's challenge. You have observed the sun rising every morning for your entire life. Does this justify the belief that it will rise tomorrow? Logically, no — the past does not guarantee the future. Every inductive inference is a leap. The problem of induction has never been solved in a philosophically satisfying way. It has merely been rendered practical: induction works, even if we cannot prove it must.

### Paradigm shifts
Thomas Kuhn's model of scientific progress. Science does not advance by steady accumulation. It goes through periods of normal science (puzzle-solving within an accepted framework) punctuated by revolutions (wholesale replacement of one framework with another). Newtonian mechanics to relativity. Ptolemaic astronomy to Copernican. The shift is not just intellectual — it changes what questions can be asked, what counts as evidence, and what the world looks like.

### Theory-ladenness of observation
The idea that there is no such thing as pure observation. What you see depends on what theories you hold. A trained radiologist sees tumours in an X-ray that a layperson cannot see. A physicist sees quantum effects in data that a non-physicist sees as noise. Observation is always filtered through theory, which means the empiricist ideal of "just look at the data" is more complicated than it appears.

### Underdetermination
Any set of observations is compatible with multiple theories. The data alone never uniquely determine the correct theory. You can always construct alternative theories that explain the same observations. This is why science needs additional criteria beyond empirical adequacy — simplicity, parsimony, explanatory power, fruitfulness.

### The demarcation problem
What distinguishes science from non-science? Popper said falsifiability. Kuhn said paradigmatic normal science. Lakatos said progressive research programmes. Feyerabend said there is no demarcation — "anything goes." The problem remains unsolved, but the attempts to solve it illuminate what makes scientific thinking distinctive.

### Reproducibility
An observation that cannot be reproduced is not evidence. The replication crisis in psychology, medicine, and other fields revealed that many published findings fail to replicate. This is not a failure of science — it is science working as intended, slowly correcting itself. But it is a reminder that single studies prove nothing.

### Controlled experiments
The gold standard of empirical evidence. Change one variable, hold everything else constant, observe the result. Randomised controlled trials in medicine. A/B testing in technology. The power of the controlled experiment is that it isolates causation from correlation. The limitation is that many important questions cannot be experimentally controlled.

### Natural experiments
Situations where nature or history provides something approximating a controlled experiment. The division of Germany into East and West. The random assignment of Vietnam War draft numbers. The staggered adoption of policies across jurisdictions. Natural experiments are less conclusive than controlled ones but often address questions that cannot be tested any other way.

### Observational studies
Studies that observe without intervening. Epidemiological studies, surveys, longitudinal cohorts. The fundamental problem: correlation does not imply causation. People who take vitamin supplements are healthier, but that may be because health-conscious people take supplements, not because supplements cause health. Confounders lurk everywhere.

### Operationalism
The idea that scientific concepts must be defined in terms of the operations used to measure them. "Intelligence is what intelligence tests measure." This sounds circular, but it enforces a useful discipline: if you cannot specify how to measure something, you do not yet know what you mean by it.

### Instrumentalism vs realism
Do scientific theories describe reality as it actually is (realism), or are they merely useful tools for prediction (instrumentalism)? Does the electron "really exist," or is it a construct that helps us predict experimental outcomes? Realists say science discovers truths about the world. Instrumentalists say science produces models that work. The debate is unresolved and may be irresolvable.

### Measurement
The act of assigning numbers to phenomena according to rules. Every measurement has error. Every measurement involves choices about what to measure and how. The history of science is partly a history of developing better measurements — better telescopes, better clocks, better sensors.

### Confirmation and disconfirmation
Evidence can confirm or disconfirm a hypothesis. But confirmation is weaker than disconfirmation — a thousand observations consistent with your theory do not prove it, but one observation inconsistent with it can refute it. This asymmetry is the basis of Popper's falsificationism and the reason science progresses by ruling things out.

### The Duhem-Quine thesis
You can never test a single hypothesis in isolation. Every test involves auxiliary assumptions — about your instruments, your experimental setup, the background conditions. When a prediction fails, you do not know which part of your theoretical web is wrong. This means individual experiments rarely settle questions definitively.

### Inference to the best explanation (abduction)
Choosing the hypothesis that best explains the observed evidence. Not deduction (which guarantees truth) and not induction (which generalises from samples), but abduction — the creative leap to the most plausible explanation. Most scientific reasoning involves abduction: Darwin did not deduce evolution from observations; he inferred it as the best explanation for the pattern of evidence.

### Bayesian confirmation theory
The modern framework for understanding how evidence supports hypotheses. Evidence confirms a hypothesis if it is more likely under that hypothesis than under the alternative. The degree of confirmation is measured by the likelihood ratio. This provides a precise, quantitative account of what it means for evidence to "support" a theory.

### The base rate neglect problem
Empirical evidence must be interpreted in context. A positive drug test is strong evidence only if the base rate of drug use is non-trivial. A diagnostic test with 99% accuracy is nearly useless if the disease it detects has a prevalence of 0.01%. Empiricism requires not just collecting evidence but interpreting it correctly — which requires probability theory.


## Key thinkers

**Aristotle** (384-322 BCE) — Greek philosopher who pioneered systematic empirical observation. Catalogued species, dissected animals, observed constitutions. His biology was more empirical than his physics. Established the idea that knowledge begins with observation, even while maintaining rationalist elements.

**Francis Bacon** (1561-1626) — English philosopher who formulated the first systematic account of the scientific method. His Novum Organum proposed inductive reasoning from carefully gathered observations as the path to knowledge. Warned against the "idols" — systematic biases that corrupt observation. Often considered the father of empiricism as a programme.

**John Locke** (1632-1704) — English philosopher who argued that the mind begins as a blank slate (tabula rasa) and all knowledge comes from experience. His Essay Concerning Human Understanding is the founding text of British empiricism. Divided experience into sensation (outer sense) and reflection (inner sense).

**David Hume** (1711-1776) — Scottish philosopher who pushed empiricism to its logical extreme. Demonstrated that causation cannot be observed — we see correlation, not cause. Formulated the problem of induction. Showed that "is" does not imply "ought." His scepticism about the foundations of empirical knowledge remains philosophically devastating and practically irrelevant — science works even if Hume is right.

**Karl Popper** (1902-1994) — Austrian-British philosopher who proposed falsifiability as the criterion for scientific theories. Argued that science advances by bold conjectures and rigorous attempts at refutation, not by accumulation of confirming evidence. His work shaped the self-understanding of 20th-century science.

**Thomas Kuhn** (1922-1996) — American philosopher of science who argued that scientific progress is not continuous but punctuated by paradigm shifts. The Structure of Scientific Revolutions (1962) changed how philosophers, historians, and scientists themselves understand the process of scientific change.

**Imre Lakatos** (1922-1974) — Hungarian philosopher who attempted to reconcile Popper and Kuhn. Proposed the methodology of scientific research programmes — clusters of theories with a "hard core" protected by auxiliary hypotheses. Research programmes are progressive (making novel predictions) or degenerating (only explaining away anomalies).

**Paul Feyerabend** (1924-1994) — Austrian philosopher who argued against all methodological rules in science. Against Method (1975) claimed that the only rule consistent with the history of science is "anything goes." Provocative and partly right: science has advanced through rule-breaking as well as rule-following.

**Willard Van Orman Quine** (1908-2000) — American philosopher who blurred the line between empiricism and rationalism. Argued that the distinction between analytic truths (true by definition) and synthetic truths (true by observation) is untenable. His "web of belief" model holds that all beliefs are revisable in light of experience, including logical and mathematical ones.


## Connections

**Probability** — Probability provides the mathematical framework for interpreting empirical evidence. Significance testing, confidence intervals, Bayesian confirmation — all are tools for extracting knowledge from observations. Empiricism without probability is anecdote.

**Rationalism** — The ancient rivalry that is actually a partnership. Rationalism provides the reasoning framework; empiricism provides the data. Pure rationalism risks building castles in the air. Pure empiricism risks drowning in data without understanding.

**Epistemology** — Empiricism is a position within epistemology — an answer to the question "where does knowledge come from?" It says: from experience. This answer raises its own questions (the problem of induction, theory-ladenness, underdetermination) that epistemology must address.

**Logic** — Logic provides the deductive structure within which empirical inferences are drawn. Modus tollens is the logical form of falsification: "If H then P; not P; therefore not H." Empirical reasoning uses logical structure even though its conclusions are never logically certain.

**Stoicism** — The Stoic emphasis on attending to what is actually happening (rather than what you wish were happening) is a proto-empiricist practice. Marcus Aurelius's discipline of seeing things "as they really are" parallels the empiricist commitment to observation over theory.

**Ethics** — Empirical ethics asks: what actually promotes well-being? Not what should in theory, but what does in practice. Evidence-based policy, randomised controlled trials in development economics, the effective altruism movement — all apply empiricist methods to ethical questions.

**Decision-making** — Good decisions require good evidence. Empiricism provides the methods for gathering and evaluating that evidence. Decision-making under uncertainty depends on the quality of the empirical inputs — how well you observed, how carefully you measured, how honestly you interpreted.

**Emotional intelligence** — Observation of others requires empirical skills — reading body language, tone, behaviour patterns. Emotional intelligence is partly the empiricism of social interaction: attending to evidence rather than projecting your assumptions.

**Algebra** — Statistical methods that process empirical data are algebraic in nature. Regression, analysis of variance, factor analysis — the mathematical machinery that turns observations into knowledge is built on algebraic structures.
