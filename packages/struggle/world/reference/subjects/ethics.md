# Ethics

## What it is

Ethics is the discipline of figuring out what you should do — not what you want
to do, not what you can do, but what you ought to do, especially when the answer
is not obvious and the stakes are real. It is the oldest and most urgent branch
of philosophy, because every action carries moral weight whether you notice it or
not.


## Core concepts

### Consequentialism

The view that the morality of an action depends entirely on its outcomes. The
right thing to do is whatever produces the best consequences. Simple in
principle, enormously difficult in practice — because consequences are hard to
predict, hard to measure, and the question of "best for whom?" opens an abyss.

### Utilitarianism

The most influential form of consequentialism. The right action is the one that
maximises total well-being (utility) across all affected parties. Bentham's
version focused on pleasure and pain; Mill refined it to distinguish higher and
lower pleasures. The strength of utilitarianism is its impartiality — everyone's
welfare counts equally. Its weakness is that it can justify horrifying things
(torture, sacrifice of innocents) if the numbers work out.

### Effective altruism

A modern movement applying utilitarian reasoning to philanthropy and career
choice. The core insight: if you're going to help, you should try to help as
much as possible per unit of resources. This means measuring impact, comparing
interventions, and sometimes making counterintuitive choices (earning to give,
prioritising neglected causes). The movement has been both praised for rigour
and criticised for cold-bloodedness and hubris.

### Deontology

The view that some actions are inherently right or wrong, regardless of their
consequences. Lying is wrong even when it produces good outcomes. Killing is
wrong even when it saves five lives. The appeal of deontology is that it
provides firm moral ground — rules that cannot be traded away for a net benefit.
Its weakness is rigidity in the face of genuine dilemmas.

### Kant's categorical imperative

The centrepiece of deontological ethics. Kant proposed several formulations, but
the core two are:

1. **Universalisability:** Act only according to a maxim that you could will to be
   a universal law. (If everyone did this, would it still work? If not, it's
   wrong.)
2. **Humanity as end:** Treat humanity, in yourself and others, always as an end
   and never merely as a means. (People are not tools.)

The categorical imperative is powerful because it provides a formal test for
moral rules. It is difficult because applying it to real situations requires
judgement about what counts as the relevant maxim.

### Rights-based ethics

The view that individuals have fundamental rights (to life, liberty, property,
self-determination) that cannot be overridden by considerations of aggregate
welfare. Rights create moral boundaries that even beneficial outcomes cannot
justify crossing. This framework underlies modern human rights law and liberal
political philosophy.

### Virtue ethics

The oldest tradition in Western ethics, rooted in Aristotle. The central
question is not "what should I do?" but "what kind of person should I be?"
Virtue ethics holds that a good life consists in developing and exercising the
virtues — courage, temperance, justice, wisdom, and others. Right action flows
from right character. The weakness is that it provides less guidance in specific
dilemmas; the strength is that it takes moral development seriously.

### Aristotle's eudaimonia

Often translated as "happiness" but better understood as "flourishing" or
"living well and doing well." For Aristotle, eudaimonia is the ultimate purpose
of human life — not a feeling but an activity, the active exercise of virtue over
a complete life. This grounds ethics in human nature and the question of what it
means to live a genuinely good life.

### MacIntyre and traditions

Alasdair MacIntyre argued that modern moral philosophy has fractured because we
have lost the shared traditions and practices that give moral concepts their
meaning. His solution: recover the Aristotelian tradition of virtue embedded in
social practices and narrative. Ethics, for MacIntyre, is not a set of abstract
rules but a living tradition sustained by communities.

### Care ethics

Developed primarily by feminist philosophers (Gilligan, Noddings, Held), care
ethics centres moral life on relationships, responsiveness, and the concrete
needs of particular others — not on abstract principles. It challenges the
assumption that impartiality is always a virtue and argues that our deepest
moral obligations arise from our connections to specific people.

### Moral particularism

The view (associated with Jonathan Dancy) that there are no fixed moral
principles — a feature that counts as a reason for action in one context might
count against it in another. Moral judgement is always context-dependent and
cannot be reduced to rules. This is not relativism (some actions are still
wrong); it is the claim that moral reasoning is more like perception than
calculation.

### Trolley problems and their limits

Philippa Foot's trolley problem — is it permissible to divert a runaway trolley
to kill one person instead of five? — and Judith Jarvis Thomson's variations
have generated decades of moral philosophy. They reveal deep tensions between
consequentialist and deontological intuitions. But they can also mislead:
real-world moral dilemmas rarely come with clean numbers and perfect information.
The trolley problem is a diagnostic tool, not a model of actual moral life.

### Moral intuitions vs moral reasoning

We all have moral intuitions — gut feelings that something is right or wrong. But
intuitions can conflict, and they can be wrong (shaped by bias, culture, or
evolutionary accident). The question is whether to trust intuition, reason, or
some reflective equilibrium between them. Rawls proposed working back and forth
between principles and intuitions until they cohere. Others (Singer, Greene)
argue we should override intuition when it conflicts with sound reasoning.

### Moral uncertainty

What should you do when you're not sure which moral theory is correct? If you're
60% consequentialist and 40% deontologist, how do you act? This is not a
theoretical curiosity — it describes the actual condition of most thoughtful
people. William MacAskill and others have developed frameworks for moral
decision-making under genuine normative uncertainty.

### The is-ought gap (Hume's guillotine)

You cannot derive an "ought" from an "is." No amount of factual information
about the world logically entails a moral conclusion. This is Hume's devastating
observation, and it remains unsolved. It means that ethics cannot be reduced to
science, and that every moral argument must at some point invoke a value that is
not itself derived from facts.

### Moral realism vs anti-realism

Are moral facts real — part of the fabric of the universe — or are they human
constructions? Moral realists (like the Cornell Realists or Derek Parfit) hold
that some moral claims are objectively true. Anti-realists (like Mackie or
Blackburn) argue that moral claims express attitudes, conventions, or
evolutionary adaptations rather than objective truths. The debate matters because
it determines whether moral disagreement is about facts or preferences.

### Supererogation

Acts that are morally praiseworthy but not obligatory. Jumping on a grenade to
save your squad is heroic, but failing to do so is not wrong. The concept marks
the boundary between duty and heroism, and raises the question of how much
morality can demand of us. Consequentialism struggles with supererogation
because if an action maximises utility, it seems obligatory.

### Moral luck

Bernard Williams and Thomas Nagel observed that moral judgement is deeply
affected by factors outside the agent's control. The drunk driver who kills a
pedestrian is judged more harshly than one who makes it home safely, even though
their recklessness was identical. Moral luck challenges the assumption that
morality should be immune to fortune — and reveals that our actual practices of
praise and blame are messier than our theories.

### The demandingness problem

How much does morality require of you? If consequentialism is right and you
should maximise overall welfare, you should give away most of your wealth, work
in the most impactful career regardless of personal preference, and sacrifice
nearly all personal projects. Most people find this implausibly demanding. But
the alternative — allowing limits on moral demands — seems to license selfishness.
There is no clean solution.


## Key thinkers

**Aristotle (384-322 BCE)** — Founded virtue ethics. Identified eudaimonia as the
ultimate good, catalogued the virtues, and established the idea that ethics is
about character, not just rules. His Nicomachean Ethics remains the starting
point for virtue ethics 2,400 years later.

**Immanuel Kant (1724-1804)** — Built the most rigorous deontological system in
the history of philosophy. The categorical imperative attempts to ground morality
in pure reason alone, independent of desire, consequence, or sentiment. His
moral philosophy is demanding, difficult, and impossible to ignore.

**John Stuart Mill (1806-1873)** — Refined utilitarianism beyond Bentham's crude
felicific calculus. Distinguished between higher and lower pleasures, defended
individual liberty, and argued for women's equality. On Liberty and
Utilitarianism remain essential.

**Jeremy Bentham (1748-1832)** — Founded modern utilitarianism. Proposed the
principle of utility — the greatest happiness of the greatest number — and
attempted to quantify pleasure and pain. His influence on law, policy, and
economics is enormous.

**John Rawls (1921-2002)** — Revived political philosophy with A Theory of
Justice. His device of the "original position" and "veil of ignorance" provides
a powerful thought experiment for deriving principles of justice: choose the
rules you'd want if you didn't know what position you'd occupy in society.

**Peter Singer (1946-)** — Applied utilitarian reasoning to animal welfare, global
poverty, and effective altruism. His work has changed real-world behaviour more
than almost any living philosopher. Controversial for his willingness to follow
arguments to uncomfortable conclusions.

**Derek Parfit (1942-2017)** — Reasons and Persons is one of the most important
works of 20th-century philosophy. Parfit explored personal identity, future
generations, moral motivation, and the convergence of ethical theories with
extraordinary rigour and clarity.

**Philippa Foot (1920-2010)** — Revived virtue ethics in analytic philosophy and
invented the trolley problem. Her work challenged the dominance of
consequentialism and deontology and showed that virtue ethics could be
philosophically serious.

**Judith Jarvis Thomson (1929-2020)** — Developed the most influential variations
on the trolley problem and wrote the classic defense of abortion rights using the
violinist thought experiment. Her work showed the power of moral intuition pumps
in philosophical argument.

**Christine Korsgaard (1952-)** — The leading contemporary Kantian. Her work on
the sources of normativity asks why moral claims have authority over us, and
answers from a constructivist Kantian perspective — we create moral obligations
through our practical identities and commitments.

**Alasdair MacIntyre (1929-2024)** — Diagnosed the fragmentation of modern moral
discourse in After Virtue. Argued for a recovery of Aristotelian tradition-based
ethics embedded in social practices. His narrative approach to ethics — we
understand our lives as stories — has been deeply influential.

**Emmanuel Levinas (1906-1995)** — Grounded ethics in the face-to-face encounter
with the Other. For Levinas, the ethical demand is not derived from theory but
arises in the irreducible confrontation with another person's vulnerability and
need. Ethics, for Levinas, is "first philosophy" — prior to ontology.


## Connections

### Probability
Consequentialism requires predicting outcomes, which means reasoning under
uncertainty. Expected value calculations are central to utilitarian reasoning.
Moral uncertainty itself is a probabilistic concept — how confident are you in
your moral framework? Risk assessment is inseparable from ethical
decision-making in any consequentialist framework.

### Rationalism
Kant's ethics is the purest expression of rationalist morality — moral law
derivable from reason alone, independent of experience. The categorical
imperative is a test of logical consistency applied to moral maxims. Deontology
in general leans rationalist: the right action is the rational action.

### Empiricism
Virtue ethics is more empirical than deontology — it asks what the virtues are by
observing what makes human lives go well. Care ethics centres on concrete
experience rather than abstract principle. Moral particularism is radically
empiricist: every moral situation must be judged on its own terms, not
subsumed under general rules.

### Epistemology
The is-ought gap is fundamentally an epistemological problem: what kind of
knowledge is moral knowledge? Moral realism claims it is factual; anti-realism
claims otherwise. Moral uncertainty is an epistemological condition applied to
normative questions. The debate between intuition and reasoning in ethics mirrors
the epistemological debate between foundationalism and coherentism.

### Logic
Kant's categorical imperative is a logical test — can this maxim be universalised
without contradiction? Moral reasoning requires logical consistency: you cannot
hold contradictory moral principles. But moral particularism challenges the idea
that moral reasoning is deductive at all, suggesting it is more like pattern
recognition than syllogism.

### Stoicism
Stoic ethics is a virtue ethics centred on the cardinal virtues. It overlaps
with Aristotelian virtue ethics but diverges in holding virtue as sufficient for
eudaimonia (Aristotle thought external goods also mattered). The Stoic
dichotomy of control shapes what you can be held morally responsible for.
Stoic cosmopolitanism is an ethical commitment — extending moral concern to all
rational beings.

### Decision-making
Ethics provides the value function for decision-making — it tells you what
counts as a good outcome. Decision theory provides the machinery for choosing
under uncertainty, but without ethical input, it has no direction. The
interaction is deep: moral intuitions often conflict with decision-theoretic
optimality, and resolving that tension is the central challenge of applied
ethics.

### Emotional intelligence
Moral judgement is not purely cognitive — emotions play a constitutive role.
Empathy drives care ethics. Moral outrage motivates justice. Compassion fatigue
limits moral action. Understanding your own emotional responses to moral
situations is essential for both good reasoning and sustainable moral practice.
Hume argued that reason is the slave of the passions, and even his critics
concede that emotion is deeply intertwined with moral life.

### Algebra
The connection is through formalisation. Utilitarian calculus attempts to reduce
moral questions to mathematical form — maximise the sum of utilities. Game theory
uses algebraic structures to model moral and strategic interactions. Social
choice theory (Arrow, Sen) applies algebra to the problem of aggregating
individual preferences into collective decisions. The limits of these
formalisations are themselves philosophically significant.
