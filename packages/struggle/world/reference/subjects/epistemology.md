# Epistemology

## What it is

Epistemology is the study of knowledge itself — what it is, how we get it, when we have it, and why it matters. It is the discipline that asks the questions that other disciplines take for granted: What counts as evidence? When is a belief justified? Can you know something without being certain of it? In the Torus, epistemology is not academic philosophy. It is the difference between confidence that is warranted and confidence that will get you killed.


## Core concepts

### Justified true belief
The classical definition of knowledge, from Plato's Theaetetus. You know something if (1) it is true, (2) you believe it, and (3) your belief is justified. This definition held for over two thousand years before Gettier demolished it. It remains the starting point for all modern epistemology.

### Gettier problems
In 1963, Edmund Gettier published a three-page paper showing that justified true belief is not sufficient for knowledge. You can have a justified belief that happens to be true, but for the wrong reasons — and this does not count as knowledge. A farmer sees what looks like a sheep in a field. It is actually a dog wearing a sheepskin. But there is a real sheep hidden behind a hill. The farmer has a justified, true belief that there is a sheep in the field — but does not know it. Gettier problems shattered the classical definition and forced epistemologists to find something better. They have not yet succeeded.

### The regress problem
If every belief must be justified by another belief, what justifies that belief? And what justifies the justifier? You face an infinite regress. Three responses:

**Foundationalism** — The chain of justification terminates in basic beliefs that are self-justifying. Descartes's "I think, therefore I am" is a candidate basic belief. Sense data might be another. The challenge is identifying which beliefs are truly basic and explaining why they need no further justification.

**Coherentism** — Beliefs are justified not by a foundation but by their coherence with other beliefs. No belief is basic; instead, beliefs support each other in a web. The challenge is that a coherent set of beliefs might be entirely divorced from reality — a coherent fiction is still fiction.

**Infinitism** — The chain of justification is infinite, and that is acceptable. Each belief is justified by another, without end, and this infinite chain constitutes adequate justification. Few philosophers accept this, but it has serious defenders.

### Internalism vs externalism
Internalism holds that justification depends on factors accessible to the believer — your evidence, your reasoning, things you can introspect on. Externalism holds that justification can depend on factors outside your awareness — the reliability of your cognitive processes, the causal history of your belief. The distinction matters practically: can you tell whether your own beliefs are justified, or might you be justified without knowing it?

### Reliabilism
An externalist theory: a belief is justified if it is produced by a reliable cognitive process — one that tends to produce true beliefs. You do not need to know the process is reliable. If your visual system reliably produces accurate beliefs about your environment, then your visual beliefs are justified, even if you cannot prove your vision is reliable. Alvin Goldman's contribution to epistemology.

### The problem of the criterion
How do you know your method for acquiring knowledge is good? You would need to use some method to evaluate it — but how do you know that method is good? Circular. This is the deepest problem in epistemology: you cannot validate your epistemic methods without already having epistemic methods. Responses range from pragmatism ("use what works") to particularism ("start with things you clearly know and work outward") to methodism ("start with criteria and apply them").

### Scepticism

**Cartesian scepticism** — Descartes's method of doubt. What if an evil demon is deceiving you about everything? What if you are a brain in a vat? You cannot rule it out using evidence, because the evidence itself might be fabricated. The sceptical challenge is not that you should believe you are deceived, but that you cannot prove you are not.

**Pyrrhonian scepticism** — The ancient Greek tradition of suspending judgment on all matters. Not asserting that knowledge is impossible (that would be a knowledge claim), but refusing to commit to any position. Achieving ataraxia (tranquility) through the suspension of judgment.

**Local scepticism** — Scepticism about specific domains rather than all knowledge. Moral scepticism (can we know moral truths?), inductive scepticism (can we know the future?), other-minds scepticism (can we know what others experience?). More targeted and often more productive than global scepticism.

### Knowledge by acquaintance vs knowledge by description
Bertrand Russell's distinction. You know London by acquaintance if you have been there — you have direct experiential contact with it. You know London by description if you have only read about it — you know it as "the capital of England." Acquaintance gives a kind of knowledge that description cannot replicate. The taste of coffee, the feeling of grief, the experience of red — these are known by acquaintance or not at all.

### A priori vs a posteriori
A priori knowledge is knowledge independent of experience — mathematical truths, logical truths, "all bachelors are unmarried." A posteriori knowledge depends on experience — "water boils at 100 degrees Celsius," "there are eight planets." The distinction seems clear until Quine challenged it, arguing that all knowledge is revisable in light of experience, including logical and mathematical truths.

### Testimony and trust
Most of what you know, you know because someone told you. You have never verified that the Earth is 4.5 billion years old. You trust the testimony of geologists, who trust the testimony of physicists, who trust the calibration of instruments. Knowledge depends on chains of testimony — which means it depends on trust. Epistemology of testimony asks: when is it rational to believe what others tell you?

### Epistemic humility
The recognition that your beliefs might be wrong — that your map might not match the territory. Not paralysing doubt, but calibrated confidence. Epistemic humility means holding beliefs proportional to evidence, acknowledging uncertainty, and remaining open to revision. It is a virtue because it tracks truth better than arrogance.

### Epistemic courage
The complement to epistemic humility. The willingness to form and defend beliefs when the evidence supports them, even when those beliefs are unpopular, uncomfortable, or threatening to your identity. Epistemic cowardice — refusing to commit to positions for fear of being wrong — is as much a failure as overconfidence.

### Epistemic virtue
The idea (from virtue epistemology) that knowledge depends not just on evidence and methods but on the character of the knower. Intellectual curiosity, open-mindedness, thoroughness, intellectual honesty, fair-mindedness — these are epistemic virtues. Their opposites — closed-mindedness, intellectual laziness, dogmatism — are epistemic vices. Zagzebski's contribution: epistemic virtues are a species of moral virtues.

### The KK principle
"If you know that P, then you know that you know that P." This seems plausible — surely if you know something, you are aware that you know it? But many epistemologists reject it. You might know things without knowing that you know them. Children know things they cannot articulate. Experts have knowledge they cannot make explicit. The KK principle collapses under pressure.

### Epistemic closure
If you know P, and you know that P implies Q, do you know Q? The principle of epistemic closure says yes. But it has counterintuitive consequences: you know you have hands; if you have hands, you are not a brain in a vat; therefore you know you are not a brain in a vat. Robert Nozick rejected closure to handle sceptical arguments. The debate continues.

### Social epistemology
Knowledge is not just an individual achievement — it is social. Science is a social process. Wikipedia is a social process. Peer review, testimony, argumentation, consensus-formation — all are social. Social epistemology asks: how do groups form knowledge? When does the group know something that no individual knows? How do social structures help or hinder the pursuit of truth?

### Epistemic injustice
Miranda Fricker's concept. Testimonial injustice occurs when a speaker's credibility is deflated because of prejudice. Hermeneutical injustice occurs when a person lacks the conceptual resources to understand or communicate their experience.


## Key thinkers

**Plato** (428-348 BCE) — Greek philosopher who defined knowledge as justified true belief in the Theaetetus. His allegory of the cave is the foundational image of epistemology: prisoners mistake shadows for reality. The philosopher escapes the cave and sees the truth — then must return to convince those who have only ever seen shadows.

**Rene Descartes** (1596-1650) — French philosopher who reinvented epistemology through radical doubt. Doubted everything until he found one thing he could not doubt: his own existence as a thinking thing. Built knowledge back up from this foundation. His method of doubt remains the paradigmatic epistemological exercise.

**David Hume** (1711-1776) — Scottish philosopher whose sceptical arguments about causation, induction, and the self remain central to epistemology. Demonstrated that our most fundamental beliefs (that the future will resemble the past, that objects cause effects) cannot be rationally justified. Proposed that custom and habit, not reason, ground our beliefs.

**Immanuel Kant** (1724-1804) — German philosopher who attempted to reconcile rationalism and empiricism. Argued that the mind structures experience through categories (causation, space, time) that are neither derived from experience nor arbitrary. Knowledge requires both sensory input and conceptual structure. His "Copernican revolution" in philosophy: the mind does not passively receive reality but actively constructs experience.

**Edmund Gettier** (1927-2021) — American philosopher who demolished the classical definition of knowledge in a three-page paper. Published almost nothing else. The paper is one of the most consequential in the history of philosophy. It showed that justified true belief is not sufficient for knowledge, and epistemology has never recovered.

**Alvin Goldman** (b. 1938) — American philosopher who developed reliabilism and helped found social epistemology. His process reliabilism holds that beliefs are justified if produced by reliable cognitive processes, shifting justification from the internal to the external.

**Ernest Sosa** (b. 1940) — Cuban-American philosopher who developed virtue epistemology. Knowledge requires not just true belief but apt belief — belief that succeeds through the exercise of intellectual virtue, not luck. His distinction between animal knowledge (reliable) and reflective knowledge (self-aware) is influential.

**Linda Zagzebski** (b. 1946) — American philosopher who developed a virtue-based account of knowledge that parallels virtue ethics. Defined knowledge as "a state of cognitive contact with reality arising out of acts of intellectual virtue." Her Gettier-style argument against all "belief + something" definitions of knowledge is powerful.

**Timothy Williamson** (b. 1955) — British philosopher who argues that knowledge is the fundamental epistemic concept — more basic than belief, justification, or evidence. Knowledge first, not belief first. Evidence is what you know. This inverts the traditional approach and has been enormously influential in contemporary epistemology.


## Connections

**Probability** — Bayesian epistemology treats degrees of belief (credences) as the fundamental epistemic unit. Knowledge is not binary (know/don't know) but graded (more or less confident). Probability provides the formal framework for epistemology's central questions about justified belief and evidence.

**Rationalism** — Rationalism is an epistemological position: the claim that reason is the primary source of knowledge. Epistemology provides the framework within which rationalism makes its case. The rationalist community's practices (calibration, updating, seeking disconfirmation) are applied epistemology.

**Empiricism** — Empiricism is the rival epistemological position: knowledge comes from experience. The rationalism-empiricism debate is epistemology's most famous argument. Kant tried to resolve it. Quine tried to dissolve it. It persists because both sides are partly right.

**Logic** — Logic provides the structure of epistemic reasoning. Epistemic logic formalises knowledge and belief using modal operators. "S knows that P" is analysed using possible worlds semantics. Logic also underpins the deductive component of justification — the chain of reasoning from evidence to conclusion.

**Stoicism** — Stoic epistemology centred on the concept of "kataleptic impression" — a perception so clear and distinct that it compels assent. The Stoic sage withholds assent from unclear impressions. This discipline of assent is a practical epistemological method: do not commit to beliefs that are not adequately supported.

**Ethics** — Epistemic ethics asks: do we have moral obligations regarding our beliefs? Is it wrong to believe without evidence? William Clifford said yes ("it is wrong always, everywhere, and for anyone, to believe anything upon insufficient evidence"). William James said no (sometimes we must decide without sufficient evidence). The debate is unresolved.

**Decision-making** — Every decision depends on what you think you know. Decision quality is bounded by epistemic quality. The best decision framework is useless if your knowledge is wrong. Epistemology provides the quality control for the inputs to decision-making.

**Emotional intelligence** — Self-knowledge — knowing your own emotional states, biases, and tendencies — is an epistemological achievement. Emotional intelligence requires the epistemic skill of accurate self-observation, which is harder than observing the external world because the observer and the observed are the same.

**Algebra** — Epistemic logic uses algebraic structures (Boolean algebras, Kripke frames) to model knowledge and belief. The algebra of knowledge includes operations like conjunction, disjunction, and negation of epistemic states. Formal epistemology is increasingly mathematical.
