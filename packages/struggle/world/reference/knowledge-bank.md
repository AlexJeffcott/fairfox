# Knowledge Bank

## Principles

All core principles are present from the start. The progression is refinement, nuance, subtlety — not difficulty. The same ideas recur with more texture, more ambiguity, more edge cases. Chapter 1 is deep and rough and fundamental. Later chapters don't get harder — they get more refined.

The challenges are not maths puzzles. They are situations that demand clear thinking. The subject areas below (algebra, physics, etc.) are part of the texture of the world — they appear inside the challenges — but the challenges themselves are about the core principles.

Nothing is ever named or labelled in the story.

# Part 1: Core Principles

These are what the challenges are actually about. Each is present from the first chapter and deepens through the story.

---

## Logic and Reasoning

If this, then that. If not this, then what?

**Raw**: Two passages. One has airflow, one doesn't. The one with airflow goes somewhere. Choose.

**Refined**: Three passages. All have airflow. One warm, one cold, one the same. Nothing is certain. Reason from what you can sense and accept you might be wrong.

**Deep**: A litany on the wall contradicts what you've observed. The litany is logical. Your observation is clear. Which do you trust?

---

## Probability and Uncertainty

You almost never know. You have to act anyway.

**Raw**: A choice with no information. Two paths. Pick one. The lesson isn't which path — it's that choosing without knowing is not a failure of thinking. It's just how it is.

**Refined**: Partial information. Signs on the walls — some reliable, some not. Bones down one path. But bones could mean that path was popular, not deadly. Evidence is not proof.

**Deep**: A pattern you relied on — warm air means safe passage — works five times. The sixth time, it doesn't. Hold on tightly, let go lightly.

---

## Observation

Seeing what's actually there. Everything else depends on this.

**Raw**: A room. Most people see the obvious thing. The important thing is small — a mark on the floor, a change in colour, a sound that's different.

**Refined**: A room that looks the same as one you've been in before. It's not. Something is different. The challenge is noticing when your brain says "I've seen this already."

**Deep**: The important information is an absence. Something that should be there and isn't. Seeing what's missing is harder than seeing what's present.

---

## Abstraction

The pattern behind the pattern.

**Raw**: Two mechanisms that look different but work the same way. The materials change but the principle doesn't.

**Refined**: A situation that looks nothing like any previous challenge but is structurally identical. The reader who recognises the underlying pattern solves it.

**Deep**: The Torus itself is an abstraction — a system encoding principles in physical form. The challenges are the same few ideas in different materials. The Torus is a language.

---

## Complexity vs Simplicity

When is the simple answer right? When is it hiding something?

**Raw**: A challenge that looks complicated. The answer is simple — one action. The complexity was noise.

**Refined**: A challenge that looks simple. The obvious action is wrong — a hidden layer changes everything.

**Deep**: A challenge where the answer is genuinely complex but can be understood simply. The skill is knowing which kind of problem you're facing.

---

## Understanding Systems

How parts connect. Cause chains. Feedback. Emergence.

**Raw**: Pull this, that moves. The room is a machine you can trace.

**Refined**: Change one thing and three things change. Dependencies you didn't see. Second-order effects.

**Deep**: The system you're in is the experiment itself. Understanding it changes her relationship to it.

---

## Inference

Drawing conclusions from incomplete information.

**Raw**: Bones near a passage. Someone went that way and died. Or someone came from that way. The bones alone don't tell you which.

**Refined**: Three pieces of evidence pointing different directions. One misleading, one irrelevant, one true. Weighing evidence, not just collecting it.

**Deep**: The correct inference is counterintuitive. The evidence points one way but the answer is the other. Base rates. Being wrong when you were sure.

---

## Emotional Intelligence

Reading yourself. Reading situations. Acting from clarity, not reaction.

**Raw**: Fear. A dark passage, a sound. The challenge is to notice the fear and then think clearly anyway. *You're afraid. That's fine. What do you actually see?*

**Refined**: The emotionally satisfying choice is wrong. Revenge, stubbornness, pride. Setting down what you feel and picking up what you know.

**Deep**: The Settlers. Reading what people feel without them saying it. Understanding why someone tries to stop her and not hating them for it.

---

## Choosing What to Believe

Epistemology as survival.

**Raw**: A litany on the wall. It sounds true. Is it? Wanting something to be true doesn't make it true. *What is true is already so.*

**Refined**: Two litanies that contradict each other. Both sound reasonable. You can't believe both. What's your evidence?

**Deep**: A litany she believed for a long time turns out to be wrong. Letting go costs something. Hold on tightly, let go lightly. The litany about litanies.

---

## Self-Knowledge

Your biases, fears, fatigue, and how they distort your thinking.

**Raw**: You're tired and you make a mistake. *You were tired. That's why. Rest first next time.* Metacognition begins.

**Refined**: You notice yourself wanting a particular answer to be true because you're afraid of the alternative. *You want the left passage because it's wider. That's not a reason. That's fear.*

**Deep**: The Torus shaped her thinking as well as her body. She has biases the Torus gave her. They served her. They're still biases.

---

## Patience vs Action

When to wait and when to move.

**Raw**: A mechanism that does something different depending on when you interact with it. Rushing kills. Waiting reveals.

**Refined**: Waiting too long is as dangerous as rushing. Water rising, opening closing. Reading the tempo.

**Deep**: The decision to leave the Settlers. She could stay longer, learn more, prepare. At what point does preparation become avoidance?

---

## Causation

Not just what happened but why.

**Raw**: A sound, then a change. Did the sound cause the change, or did something else cause both?

**Refined**: A pattern that looks causal but isn't. Correlation masquerading as causation.

**Deep**: Why the Torus is the way it is. Why these challenges? Why this shape? Causation applied to the system itself.

---

## Perspective

The same thing looks different from a different angle.

**Raw**: A mark on the wall that makes no sense until you look at it from across the room.

**Refined**: A challenge she failed once. She comes back after other experiences. It looks different. Not because it changed — because she did.

**Deep**: The Settlers' perspective. Why they stopped. It's not weakness. Her drive isn't strength. They're perspectives.

---

## Resourcefulness

Working with what you have.

**Raw**: A gap to cross and nothing to cross it with — except the things you assumed were debris.

**Refined**: Combining things that don't obviously go together.

**Deep**: Her own memory, the litanies she carries, the patterns she's recognised. The most powerful tool is everything she's already learned.

---

## Attention

What you notice and what you miss.

**Raw**: Something important in a room full of distractions.

**Refined**: The important thing isn't an object — it's a change. Temperature shifted. Sound different. Air wrong.

**Deep**: Attending to your own thinking. Noticing when you're rationalising. Noticing when you're avoiding. Attention turned inward.

# Part 2: Subject Knowledge

The texture of the world. These aren't what the challenges are *about* — they're the material the challenges are *made of*. The Torus is a physical place. The thinking skills above are exercised through situations that involve real-world knowledge. This section catalogues that knowledge by domain. Items within each domain don't follow a strict age progression — they appear whenever the story needs them, with increasing refinement.

---

## Algebra and Number

- Equivalence: two things that are the same in a non-obvious way
- Substitution: if A = B, you can replace A with B
- Unknowns: something is hidden, work out what it is from what you can see
- Patterns in sequences: what comes next, and why
- Proportional and inverse relationships
- Balancing: both sides must be equal
- Linear relationships: things that change at a constant rate
- Systems: two unknowns, two relationships, one answer
- Functions as machines: input, output, predictably
- Rates of change
- Linear equations as spatial problems: corridors that slope, paths that converge, solving for x with your feet
- Non-linear relationships: exponential growth, logarithmic scales
- Recursive structures: things defined in terms of themselves
- Symmetry as a mathematical concept

## Geometry and Space

- Shapes and properties: triangles are rigid, squares are not
- Angles, symmetry, area, spatial reasoning, scale
- Pythagoras as a physical fact
- Coordinate thinking: describing position with numbers
- Transformations: what's preserved when you translate, rotate, reflect
- Spatial algebra: the geometry of the Torus is the algebra. Gradients are slopes you walk on
- Three-dimensional reasoning: volumes, cross-sections, projection
- Vectors: direction and magnitude as one idea
- Non-Euclidean hints: the Torus itself curves
- Topology: a torus is not a sphere. What stays the same when you stretch and bend

## Physics and Engineering

- Forces: push, pull, gravity
- Simple machines: lever, pulley, inclined plane. Trade distance for force
- Heat: expansion, contraction, flow
- Water: flows downhill, exerts pressure with depth
- Materials: what breaks and what bends
- Energy conservation
- Momentum, pressure, fluid behaviour
- Structural engineering: what holds weight and why
- Waves: sound, light, interference, resonance
- Thermodynamics: entropy, irreversibility
- Feedback loops

## Memory and Learning

- Chunking, association, spatial memory
- Attention as the first step of memory
- Method of loci: the Torus itself as memory palace
- Spaced repetition: earlier concepts return in new forms
- Pattern vs rote: remember the rule, not every instance
- Working memory: knowing what to hold and what to let go
- Memory is reconstruction, not playback — it can be wrong
- External memory: marks on walls, litanies, the bracer itself
- **Formal memory systems**: the Dominic system (numbers → letters → people → actions), peg systems, PAO (person-action-object). These are powerful, teachable, and almost nobody uses them. The protagonist builds memory systems deliberately — not because the Torus tells her to, but because she needs to remember and her raw memory isn't enough
- Memory palaces built from real places: the Torus is ideal — she walks the same corridors, builds associations into the architecture she already knows

## Notation

- Notation as tool: symbols that compress complex ideas into manipulable form
- Mathematical notation: variables, operators, equality, inequality, functions
- Logical notation: and, or, not, implies, for all, there exists
- The power of writing things down: a mark on the wall is an external mind
- Notation is not decoration — it's what makes hard thinking possible. You can't hold a system of five relationships in your head. You can hold it on a wall
- The litanies are notation too — compressed wisdom in memorable form
- Inventing notation: when no symbol exists for what you need, make one. The bracer patterns may be a notation she doesn't yet understand

## Estimation

- Eyeballing: bigger or smaller, by how much
- Comparison as measurement: arm-lengths, body-widths
- Upper and lower bounds
- Decomposition: break a big unknown into smaller knowable pieces
- Order of magnitude: get the size right before worrying about precision
- Rates and accumulation
- Sanity checking: does this answer make sense?
- Estimation under time pressure
- Calibration: how good are your estimates? Adjust

## Error Detection

- Something looks different — what changed?
- Too easy: you're probably missing something
- Counting errors, off-by-one
- Anchoring: the first thing you notice isn't always the most important
- Gut check: is this the right size, shape, kind of answer?
- False patterns: seeing order in randomness
- Gambler's fallacy, sunk cost, survivorship bias
- Hasty generalisation
- Base rate neglect
- Availability bias, regression to the mean
- Dimensional analysis
- Adversarial thinking: what was the designer expecting you to do wrong?
- Unknown unknowns
- Meta-error: being wrong about your confidence

# Part 3: Cross-Cutting

These are not separate subjects. They're facets of the same thing. Every challenge uses several at once. A single room might test observation, inference, emotional regulation, and patience simultaneously. The reader doesn't learn them in isolation. They learn them tangled together, the way real thinking works.

Recurring structural patterns:
- **Equivalence**: appears in algebra, logic, physics, and perspective
- **Symmetry**: appears in geometry, algebra, systems, and the Torus itself
- **Feedback**: appears in systems, physics, probability, and self-knowledge
- **Abstraction**: the deepest skill — recognising the same structure in different surfaces
- **Trade-offs**: appear everywhere — strength vs weight, risk vs reward, certainty vs speed, patience vs action
- **Space as mathematics**: the Torus makes algebra physical. Navigation *is* mathematics
- **Intuition over override**: the goal is not to teach people to fight their biases but to make correct thinking feel natural. Counterintuitive truths should come to feel obvious. Intuitive falsehoods should come to feel absurd. The Torus doesn't train people to think harder — it breeds people who think *correctly by default*
