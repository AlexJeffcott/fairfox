# Inspirations

Mythic and literary sources that resonate with the story's core movement: emergence from darkness into light, from confinement to understanding, at great cost.

## Plato's Cave (Republic VII)

Prisoners chained since childhood see only shadows on a wall and take them for reality. One is freed, dragged up "the steep and rugged ascent" into sunlight, and at first he is blinded. His eyes adjust gradually: first shadows, then reflections in water, then things themselves, and at last the sun, which he comes to understand as "the cause of all things" (516b–c). The escape is not a liberation that happens to him. It is painful, and at every stage he wants to turn back. "The man would rather suffer anything than entertain these false notions and live in this miserable manner" only after the adjustment is complete. Before that, he resists. The ascent out of darkness is work done against every instinct.

**Resonance:** The Torus IS the cave and the ascent at once. The progression from soot-thick early chambers to pristine untouched stone is the progression from shadows to sunlight. The eyes adjusting gradually — that's the chapter rhythm. Each killing challenge is a threshold of pain she'd rather not cross.

## Orpheus (Virgil, Georgics IV; Ovid, Metamorphoses X)

Orpheus descends to retrieve Eurydice. His singing moves even the dead. The lords of the underworld relent, on one condition: he must not look back. They walk upward, and he has already escaped every danger — *iamque pedem referens casus evaserat omnes* — when madness seizes him. He looks back. *Ibi omnis effusus labor* — all his effort poured out, wasted. Eurydice's last words carry no reproach, only bewilderment: "What madness, Orpheus, what great madness has destroyed both you and me?"

**Resonance:** The forward-only movement. She cannot look back — not because of a prohibition but because the doors seal behind her. The memory palace is the only looking back she has, and memories are reconstructions, not the thing itself. The Settlers are people who looked back — who found a comfortable chamber and stopped.

## Aeneas (Virgil, Aeneid VI)

The Sibyl warns: *facilis descensus Averno* — the descent is easy. *Sed revocare gradum superasque evadere ad auras, hoc opus, hic labor est* — but to retrace your steps and escape to the upper air, that is the task, that is the labour. Aeneas carries the golden bough, the token that lets him pass living among the dead. He meets Dido, who will not speak to him. He meets his father, who shows him the future. He returns by the Gate of Ivory — the gate of false dreams. The descent was easy. The return was the work.

**Resonance:** The bracer as golden bough — a token that lets her pass where others cannot. The killing challenges as labour, not adventure. The Torus itself as a kind of underworld where the dead (bones of previous generations) surround the living. And the ambiguity of the Ivory Gate: what she learns in the Torus may not be the whole truth.

## Theseus and the Labyrinth (Ovid, Metamorphoses VIII; Catullus 64; Plutarch)

The labyrinth was built to be inescapable — *tecti turbata per ambages*, confused by winding passages. Theseus could kill the Minotaur; that was the lesser problem. The greater one was the dark itself, the corridors folding back until direction lost all meaning. Ariadne gave him a ball of thread — the *clew* — so he could tie one end at the entrance and unwind it as he walked. He made his way back *tenui filo* — by a thin thread. The word that recurs across every telling is *tenuis*: thin, fine, almost nothing. Strength got him to the centre. Only the thread got him out. And the thread was not his. It was given by someone who loved him, devised by someone who built the trap.

**Resonance:** The bracer's engraved lines are a thread — but one that records the path rather than leading back along it. She can't follow them home. They're proof of where she's been, not a way to return. The thread is cut behind her at every killing challenge door. The labyrinth resonates with the Torus's structure: winding, folding, designed to be inescapable except by one who proves capable. And the question of who placed the bracer on her arm — who is her Ariadne?

## Inanna's Descent (Sumerian)

The oldest underworld journey. Inanna, Queen of Heaven, descends to the Great Below dressed in all her powers — crown, lapis beads, royal robe, breastplate, gold ring. At each of the seven gates, the gatekeeper demands she remove one piece. By the seventh gate she is naked. She stands before her sister Ereshkigal stripped of everything, and Ereshkigal fastens on her the eye of death, strikes her, and hangs her corpse on a hook. She is rescued not by her own strength but by the foresight of her servant Ninshubur, who mourned at the gates above and pleaded with the gods. Even then: *"No one ascends from the underworld unmarked."* The demons followed her back, demanding a substitute. Her husband Dumuzi was dragged down in her place. Inanna came back from the dead, but she did not come back whole.

**Resonance:** The stripping at each gate. The Torus doesn't strip her of possessions — she has none — but each killing challenge strips away an assumption, a comfort, a way of thinking that worked before. And the law that no one ascends unmarked: the bracer IS the mark. The engraving is the record of what the passage cost. Also: the rescue came from outside, from relationship and foresight, not from the hero's own power. Who is her Ninshubur?

## Izanagi and Yomi (Kojiki)

Izanagi follows his dead wife Izanami to Yomi. She tells him she has eaten the food of the dead and cannot simply leave, but she will ask the gods of Yomi. He must not look at her. He waits, cannot bear the waiting, lights a tooth from his comb as a torch — and sees her body full of maggots and thunder. He flees. She pursues. He blocks the pass between worlds with a boulder. From the other side, Izanami vows to strangle one thousand of his people each day. He answers: he will build one thousand five hundred birthing huts. The marriage ends across a sealed stone with a vow of mutual destruction.

**Resonance:** The looking. The sealed door. The vow across the threshold — one side destroying, the other creating, and neither able to reach the other. The killing challenges seal behind her the same way. And the impulse to look back, to check, to need to see — this is the Settlers' condition. They stopped because they couldn't bear not knowing what they were leaving behind.

## The Harrowing of Hell (Eastern Orthodox)

The central icon of Easter in the Orthodox tradition: Christ standing on the shattered gates of Hell, reaching down to pull Adam and Eve out of their tombs by the wrist. Hell speaks as a character, a warden afraid. The doors break. All the dead who were bound are loosed from their chains. Christ's words to Adam: *"Awake, O sleeper, and arise from the dead."* The rescue comes from outside, is violent, and leaves the prison in ruins.

**Resonance:** The rescue from outside. The Torus was built by someone — the Architect — and the bracer was placed on her arm before she was conscious. Someone designed this passage. The structure is a prison that is also a school, and the question of whether she is being rescued or tested is never resolved. Also: the violence of the breaking. Each killing challenge door doesn't open gently — it simply isn't there, then is. The architecture rearranges itself. Something with power over the structure intervenes.

## The Hero Twins and Xibalba (Popol Vuh)

Two brothers are summoned to the underworld and fail every test. Their sons, the Hero Twins, are summoned in turn — but go prepared. They survive Dark House, Razor House, Cold House, Jaguar House, Fire House, Bat House through cunning. When the lords of death kill them and grind their bones, the Twins return as catfish, then as wandering performers. They sacrifice themselves before the lords of Xibalba, die, and come back. The lords demand the trick be done on them. The Twins oblige — and do not bring them back. They did not fight death. They let themselves be killed, came back, and tricked death into volunteering for its own destruction.

**Resonance:** The named houses of torment are the chapters. Dark House, Razor House, Cold House — each a specific trial with specific rules. And the Twins' method: not strength but cunning, not fighting but understanding the rules well enough to subvert them. This is exactly how the killing challenges work. The balance puzzle isn't a fight — it's comprehension. Also: the first generation fails and the second learns from their failure. The bones she passes are the first brothers. She is the Twins — inheriting the knowledge of those who came before, going prepared where they went blind.

## Savitri and Yama (Mahabharata)

Savitri chose her husband knowing he would die in one year. When Yama came, she followed him into the realm of death. Yama told her to turn back. She did not. Yama offered her boons — anything except her husband's life. She asked for her father-in-law's eyesight, his lost kingdom, a hundred sons for her father. Each granted. Then she asked for a hundred sons for herself. Yama granted it — and Savitri answered: *"How shall I have a hundred sons if my husband is dead?"* Yama, caught by his own word, released Satyavan's soul. The whole exchange is conducted while walking, step by step, into the realm of death. She carries no weapon, no bough, no thread. She carries only the refusal to stop following, and a clarity of mind that turns death's own generosity into the instrument of her husband's return.

**Resonance:** The most important source for the inner voice. Savitri doesn't fight, doesn't trick, doesn't flee. She *thinks clearly under pressure* — which is the core skill the Torus selects for. Her weapon is precision of language and thought. Each boon is a premise in a logical argument whose conclusion Yama doesn't see coming until it's too late. This is what the killing challenges should feel like at their best: not tests of knowledge but tests of clear thinking, where the answer was available all along to anyone willing to follow the logic.

## Dante's Purgatorio

The *Inferno* ends in darkness. The *Purgatorio* begins with the aftermath: hoisting sails over better waters. But the mountain must be climbed terrace by terrace. On the first slope Dante is already gasping. Virgil: *"Questa montagna è tale, che sempre al cominciar di sotto è grave; e quant' om più va sù, e men fa male"* — the climb is hardest at the bottom; the higher one goes, the less it hurts. The pain lessens not because the mountain changes but because the climber does. Each terrace purges a weight — pride, envy, wrath, sloth — and with each weight shed the climbing becomes lighter. At the summit, Virgil's last words: *"Non aspettar mio dir più né mio cenno; libero, dritto e sano è tuo arbitrio"* — "Await no more word or sign from me; your will is free, upright, and whole." Both canticles end with *stelle* — stars — but the difference is everything. At the end of the *Inferno* he merely sees them. At the end of the *Purgatorio* he is *"puro e disposto a salire a le stelle"* — pure and ready to rise to them. The seeing has changed because the seer has.

**Resonance:** The Torus IS Purgatory — a mountain climbed terrace by terrace, each level purging a limitation. The climb hardest at the bottom, easiest near the top, not because the challenges get simpler but because she gets better. Virgil's farewell is the story's ending: the guide (the inner voice? the bracer? the Architect?) falls away when the will is free.

## Augustine's Confessions

Not ignorance but a divided will. Augustine knew what was true and could not make himself act on it. *"I was bound not by an iron chain imposed by anyone else, but by the iron of my own will."* The garden scene in Milan: weeping under a fig tree, a child's voice — *tolle lege* — he reads Paul, reads no further. *"Nec ultra volui legere, nec opus erat."* The breakthrough is not a triumph; it is a surrender. Years later: *"Sero te amavi, pulchritudo tam antiqua et tam nova!"* — "Late have I loved you, O Beauty ever ancient, ever new!" And: *"You were within me, and I was outside. You called, you shouted, you broke through my deafness. You flared, you blazed, you drove away my blindness."* The light was not distant. It had been there all along. What took so long was his willingness to open his eyes.

**Resonance:** The divided will is the Settlers' condition — knowing the way forward, unable to make themselves take it. And "the light was there all along" — the Torus's challenges don't add knowledge from outside. They reveal what the structure already contains. The bracer was on her arm from the start.

## St John of the Cross

Written after nine months in a cell barely six feet wide. The poem: *"En una noche oscura, con ansias, en amores inflamada, ¡oh dichosa ventura!, salí sin ser notada, estando ya mi casa sosegada"* — the soul escapes its own house in darkness, and the darkness is not the obstacle. It is the way. Three phases, each darker than the last. *"God perceives the imperfections within us, and because of his love for us, urges us to grow up. His love is not content to leave us in our weakness."* The soul feels abandoned — and that feeling IS the work being done. The radical claim: the soul sees better in the dark, because in the light it sees only itself. The last stanza arrives at stillness: *"I stayed, lost to myself, I laid my face against the Beloved's; all ceased, and I let go."* The struggle is over not because the darkness has lifted but because the soul has stopped needing it to lift.

**Resonance:** The darkness as method, not obstacle. The early chambers are dark not because the Torus is hostile but because darkness is how learning begins — in the dark you must reach for what you cannot see. The Torus doesn't light up to help her. She learns to see in the dark. Also: "the soul sees only itself in the light" — the self-gazing trap. The story must avoid this.

## Exodus

Begins in slavery, in bricks and drowned sons. The promise from the burning bush is not comfort but motion: *"I am come down to deliver them."* Ten plagues, the last in a night so total the Israelites must mark their doors with blood. They leave in haste. The crossing is not the end — barely the beginning. Forty years of wilderness follow. The people complain. *"Would to God we had died in the land of Egypt, when we sat by the flesh pots."* They would rather have slavery with food than freedom with hunger. Moses leads them through thirst, rebellion, apostasy. He never enters the promised land. He sees it from Nebo and dies on the mountain. The escape from darkness is not a single act. It is a generation's work, and the one who began it does not live to see it finished.

**Resonance:** The generation's work — each generation of the Torus inherits the bones and marks of the previous. Moses never entering is devastating: the possibility that she won't reach the end, that her progress is for whoever comes next. The flesh pots of Egypt are the Settlers' comfort — they stopped because freedom was hungry. And the forty years of circling the same ground: the spaced repetition of the Torus, revisiting the same concepts in new forms.

## Frederick Douglass

The awakening as a story about reading. Mr Auld forbade his wife from teaching Douglass the alphabet: *"Learning would spoil the best nigger in the world."* Douglass heard this as revelation: *"From that moment, I understood the pathway from slavery to freedom."* He learned in secret. *"The more I read, the more I was led to abhor and detest my enslavers."* Knowledge was not comfort. *"Learning to read had been a curse rather than a blessing. It had given me a view of my wretched condition, without the remedy."* Light brings suffering before freedom — Plato's cave, lived.

The fight with Covey the slave-breaker. Six months of being broken: *"My intellect languished, the disposition to read departed."* Then Covey attacked and Douglass fought back. Two hours. Covey could not beat him. *"You have seen how a man was made a slave; you shall see how a slave was made a man."* And: *"It was a glorious resurrection, from the tomb of slavery, to the heaven of freedom."* He was not yet free. But the darkness had broken.

**Resonance:** The most direct parallel to the Torus's design. Knowledge as the pathway from confinement to freedom — and knowledge as pain before it is power. The litanies on the walls are Douglass's alphabet: dangerous, transformative, not given freely. And the moment of physical resistance — the killing challenge that changes everything, not because you survive it but because you refuse to submit.

## Boethius

Written in a prison cell in Pavia, awaiting execution. The book opens in self-pity. Philosophy appears, dismisses the weeping Muses: *"Who has allowed these theatrical tarts to approach this sick man's bedside?"* She tells Boethius he has forgotten who he is. The entire book is the process of remembering.

On fortune: *"What is it about human misery that makes you cling to it so?"* On the wheel: *"If you are trying to stop her wheel from turning, you are the most foolish of men."* The wheel cannot be stopped at the top. Expecting it to stop is the source of suffering. Through five books Philosophy brings him to see providence where he saw fate — *"the unfolding of all things in time is, when seen in the mind of God, simply Providence; but when that same unfolding is seen in relation to the things it moves, it has been called Fate."* The prisoner cannot change his circumstances. He can change what he sees. Boethius was executed shortly after finishing the book. The light he found did not save his life. It saved everything else.

**Resonance:** The Consolation's structure IS the Torus — a prison that becomes a school through the act of thinking clearly within it. Philosophy's dismissal of the Muses: the inner voice's refusal of self-pity. "You have forgotten who you are" — but our protagonist has never known. She's building the knowledge Boethius is recovering. And the final devastating fact: the light did not save his life. The Torus may not lead to freedom. It may lead to understanding, which is not the same thing.

## The Buddha Under the Bodhi Tree (Buddhacarita; Pali Canon)

Siddhartha sits and resolves not to rise until he has seen through. Mara sends armies, then his daughters, then challenges his right to the seat. Siddhartha does not move. He touches the earth, and the earth testifies. In three watches he sees all his previous lives, all beings dying and being reborn, and the chain of dependent origination. At dawn: *"Ignorance was dispelled, knowledge arose; darkness was dispelled, light arose — as happens in one who abides diligent, ardent, and resolute."* The phrasing is almost clinical. The struggle was real, but the one who sat through it did not struggle. He stayed still, and let the darkness exhaust itself against him.

**Resonance:** The stillness at the centre. The killing challenges demand action — but the deeper lesson may be knowing when NOT to act. The earth-touching gesture: when challenged, she proves her right to be there not by argument but by the accumulated evidence of the passage itself — the bracer's engraving. And Mara's three attacks map to the Torus's three kinds of obstacle: force (the armies), desire (the daughters), and doubt (the challenge to legitimacy).

## Attar's Conference of the Birds (Mantiq al-Tayr)

Thousands of birds set out to find their king, the Simorgh, across seven valleys. Most turn back. Thirty arrive, ragged and diminished. They look into the Simorgh's face and see themselves. *Si murgh* means "thirty birds." The journey did not take them to something else. It stripped away everything that was not them, until only the essential remained.

**Resonance:** The Torus as the seven valleys — Quest, Love, Knowledge, Detachment, Unity, Bewilderment, Annihilation. The Settlers are the birds who turned back. And the ending: what waits at the end of the Torus may not be a destination. It may be a mirror. The bracer's engraving, fully complete, may describe not a map but the one who carries it.

## Milarepa (Tsangnyön Heruka)

A murderer seeks a teacher. Marpa accepts him but will not teach. Instead: build a tower. Tear it down. Carry every stone back. Build again. Tear down. Again. Milarepa's back becomes a single open sore. The purpose: each tower was a purification. Thirty-five deaths required the body's suffering, stone by stone. Only after the towers did Marpa teach. Milarepa went into the mountains, ate nettles until his skin turned green, and composed songs of extraordinary light: *"In the monastery of my body, in the temple of my breast, on the altar of my heart — the lamp of understanding burns bright."*

**Resonance:** The killing challenges as towers built and torn down. Each death restarts the chapter — the same labour, the same stones, carried again. The Torus doesn't explain why. Marpa didn't explain why. The cruelty IS the teaching, and the teaching cannot be separated from the suffering without losing what makes it work. Also: Dakmema's role — "your teacher's actions have a purpose you cannot yet see." Is there a Dakmema in the Torus?

## Sundiata (D.T. Niane / Djeli Mamoudou Kouyaté)

A child who cannot walk. Mocked, dismissed, invisible. Then: his mother weeps, he sends for an iron rod, grips it, pulls himself upright — and the rod bends. He is too strong for iron. He uproots a baobab tree and carries it to his mother's door. Years of exile follow. He returns and founds an empire. But the image the griots return to is not the battle. It is the boy on the ground, gripping the rod, rising.

**Resonance:** The first rising. Chapter 1 is this — she is on the ground, in the dark, and she stands. The iron rod bending: the bracer's first engraving. The moment everything changes is not the culmination but the beginning. And the griots' focus on the origin rather than the triumph — the story is about what it cost to rise, not what was built after.

## Rumi's Masnavi

The reed flute cut from its reedbed. The sound it makes is the sound of separation. *"The wound is the place where the Light enters you."* The falcon hooded on the wrist of the world, forgetting the sky. Sometimes the hood slips, and the longing to fly becomes greater than the fear of flight. *"What you seek is seeking you."* The work is not to travel a great distance. The work is to stop consenting to the blindfold. *"This is Love: to fly heavenward, to rend every instant a hundred veils."* Not one veil. A hundred. And not once. Every instant.

**Resonance:** The Torus as blindfold, not prison. She is not trapped — she is hooded. Each challenge is a veil rent. The litanies on the walls are the songs of those who came before, each one a way of saying the same thing from a different angle. And "what you seek is seeking you" — the Architect designed the Torus, but the Torus may also be seeking her. The bracer was waiting.

## The Egyptian Book of the Dead (Pert em Hru)

Not a book about death. A book about going forth by day. The soul navigates the Duat through gates guarded by beings whose names must be spoken correctly. To know the name of a thing is to have power over it. The culmination: the heart weighed against the feather of Ma'at. Forty-two denials in the Negative Confession. If the heart balances, the Field of Reeds. The hymn upon emerging: *"Hail to you, Ra, at your rising, at your beauty, at your shining."* The whole ordeal was for this: to stand in the sun again.

**Resonance:** The most direct structural parallel. The gates of the Duat ARE the killing challenges — each guarded, each requiring specific knowledge to pass. The Negative Confession is an inversion of the bracer: instead of proving what you've done, you prove what you haven't. But the principle is the same — the passage demands an accounting. The weighing of the heart against Ma'at is the ultimate killing challenge: not strength, not cunning, but truth. And the title — Going Forth by Day — is the title of the whole story.

## Common Grammar

What these sources share:

- **Darkness as method, not obstacle.** The dark is where the work happens. Plato's eyes must adjust. John's soul sees better without light. The falcon is hooded. The Torus is dark because darkness is how learning begins.
- **Emergence as labour.** Not gift, not rescue, not luck. The ascent is always harder than the descent. Dante's mountain, Aeneas's return, the forty years of wilderness, Milarepa's towers.
- **The cost of looking back.** Orpheus, Izanagi, the flesh pots of Egypt. The impulse to return to what was familiar defeats the passage.
- **Something carried through.** The golden bough, the thread, the adjusted eyes, the bracer, Savitri's clarity, Douglass's alphabet, the earth-touching gesture. A token, a skill, or a refusal.
- **No one returns unmarked.** Inanna's law. The bracer's engraving. Douglass's resurrection. The passage changes the one who passes.
- **The guide falls away.** Virgil leaves Dante. The thread is cut. Moses dies on Nebo. Marpa stops building towers. At some point you walk alone.
- **Knowledge as pain before power.** Douglass: "Learning to read had been a curse." Plato: the freed prisoner resists at every stage. Boethius: the light did not save his life. The Torus teaches, and teaching hurts.
- **Stripping to the essential.** Inanna at the seven gates. The thirty birds arriving ragged. Sundiata on the ground before he rises. The journey removes what is not needed, sometimes violently.
- **Stillness as mastery.** The Buddha under the tree. Savitri walking calmly into death. The darkness exhausts itself against the one who does not move.
- **The destination as mirror.** The Simorgh is the thirty birds. The Torus's end may not be an exit but a recognition. The bracer's completed engraving may describe the one who carries it.
- **The question of the designer.** Who built the cave? Who made the labyrinth? Who placed the bracer on her arm? The Architect is Daedalus, Minos, and Ariadne at once — builder, jailer, and the one who gave the thread.
