# The Experiment

## The Problem

Human intelligence evolved on the African savannah. It is optimised for social navigation, predator avoidance, resource location, and pattern recognition in natural environments. The problems of physics, mathematics, abstract logic, and higher-dimensional reasoning are alien to the architecture of the human brain. Humans can do these things — but only with enormous effort, specialised training, and constant friction against their own cognitive grain.

But intelligence isn't the only limitation. The human body is a compromise — good enough for the savannah, inadequate for what comes next.

## The Solution

An unknown intelligence — referred to here as the Architect — built the Torus and set it running. The Architect does not take a direct hand. There is no gene editing, no manipulation between generations. The selection is entirely environmental — the Torus is a structure that kills you for any weakness, and whatever gets furthest provides the genetic material for the next generation. Natural selection with an artificial environment.

The pressures are total. Not just cognitive but physical, immunological, sensory, everything. The whole organism, shaped across every axis by what the environment demands.

The deepest aim is not intelligence but *intuition*. Human cognitive biases evolved for the savannah — they are intuitions that were once correct and are now wrong. The Torus selects for humans whose gut instincts align with reality as it actually is. Things that are true but counterintuitive to us should feel obvious to them. Things that are false but intuitive to us should feel absurd to them. The goal is not humans who can override their biases with effort, but humans who don't have those biases in the first place.

The vehicle is the Torus — an enormous toroidal structure, large enough to support life. The challenges are real and physical. Failure is death.

## The Process

1. A subject is grown in an artificial womb at the origin point
2. The subject is decanted with implanted aptitude — not knowledge, but the capacity to learn fast and an instinctive physical competence. Like waking up able to do things without knowing why
3. Early chambers are quality control — checking basic abilities, weeding out regressions and rejects quickly. Most historical failures died here
4. Beyond the checks, the real challenges begin. The Torus provides conditionally — demonstrate capability and it gives you what you need. Food for puzzles. Light a fire, a fountain fills with water
5. If the subject dies, their genetic material informs the next generation
6. Remains are left where they fall

This has been going on for a very long time. The feeling is of deep time — the infrastructure is ancient, degraded, barely functional in places.

## The Settlers

Some subjects survived but got stuck. They found exploits — wrong turnings, side passages, places where they could access supplies without progressing. They settled. Not necessarily because they wanted to. Injury, fear, love, exhaustion, bad luck. Varied, human reasons.

They are encountered late in the story and are a relatively small part of it. They are not a thematic device. They're just people, living the only way available to them in the only place they've ever known.

## The Architect

We know nothing about the Architect. Not what it is, not why it did this, not whether it's still watching. Keep it open.

## The Didactic Layer

The Torus teaches. Not explicitly — it never labels anything — but the challenges are structured so that surviving them requires understanding real principles. Algebra, geometry, logic, reasoning, physics, engineering, science, mathematics. The subjects who got furthest are the ones who learned fastest.

The reader learns alongside the protagonist. The challenges are real problems with real concepts behind them — the reader has to think to progress. Get it wrong, you die, you restart the chapter.

## The Litanies

Scratched into walls throughout the Torus — left by predecessors who figured something out and left it behind. Not instructions for specific puzzles but principles of thinking. Epistemology. How to reason clearly, how to update beliefs, how to avoid fooling yourself.

Drawn from the rationalist tradition (Tarski, Bayesian reasoning, the sequences) and wider sources (Stoic maxims, scientific principles, philosophical razors). Some may contradict each other. Different people, different generations, different conclusions. Some may be wrong.

They are the most human thing in the Torus — someone's handwriting on a wall.

## Open Questions

- What is the Architect? Human, posthuman, AI, alien, collective, something else?
- Is the Architect still present or did it set this in motion and leave?
- What is outside the Torus?
- How many generations? (Enough that the early chambers are ancient and worn. The exact number doesn't matter — the feeling of deep time does)
- What does "inheriting the world" actually mean?
