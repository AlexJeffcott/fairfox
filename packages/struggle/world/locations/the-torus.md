# The Torus

## Description
A vast toroidal structure. Enormous — large enough to support life. Not a clean sequence of numbered rooms but a complex, sprawling space with main paths, wrong turnings, dead ends, and side passages. Some areas the Architect intended. Some may not have been intended at all.

## Decay Through Use

The defining environmental detail: the early sections are filthy — not decayed but used. The structure itself endures. Whatever the Torus is made of doesn't rust, doesn't corrode, doesn't break down. It was built to last geological time. But thousands of people have passed through. Hands on walls, feet on floors, bodies living and dying. The grime is human residue — ash, skin, soot, grease, dust. The surfaces underneath are intact. The technology that powers the Torus is beyond comprehension. Not broken or primitive — so advanced it looks like physics. Walls have openings when they need openings. Water appears. The bracer's surface changes. None of it can be explained. There are no visible moving parts, no doors, no hinges, no mechanisms you can watch operate. You look away, you look back, and there is a passage where there wasn't one. The Torus doesn't perform for you. It just changes state, like matter cooling from liquid to solid. The dirt and the detritus are human. Everything else just is.

Further in, fewer people made it. The wear thins out. Surfaces go from polished-by-touch to untouched. The deepest sections look almost new — pristine, waiting, never used by a human being.

The moment the wear stops is quietly terrifying.

## Structure

**Quality control (early)**: The first challenges check basic abilities — weeding out regressions and rejects. Not real tests, just filters. This is where the bones are densest. Most historical failures died here before getting started.

**Survival (early-mid)**: The first real lessons. Water, shelter, food. Primal. The cognitive challenges come later, once you've survived long enough to encounter them.

**The real challenges (mid-deep)**: Foundational concepts made physical. Algebra as balance mechanisms, geometry as spatial navigation, logic as doors that only open when you reason correctly. The challenges build — each draws on what earlier ones taught, and revisits older concepts in new forms.

**Beyond all precedent (deep)**: Where no one has been before. Clean walls, untouched surfaces, silence. Whatever is here has been waiting.

## The Litanies

Throughout the Torus, scratched into walls by predecessors. Not solutions to specific challenges but ways of thinking — epistemological principles left behind by people who figured something out. Rationalist tradition (Tarski's litany, Bayesian updating, Ockham's razor), Stoic maxims, scientific principles. Some may be wrong. Some may contradict each other. They are the accumulated wisdom — and errors — of generations.

## The Walls As Record

The litanies are part of a broader texture. The walls carry marks, scratches, crude drawings, tally marks, handprints, warnings. Some meaningful, some just evidence that someone was here. The Torus is layered with human traces that thin out as you go deeper, until the walls are unmarked.

## Transactional

The Torus provides, but conditionally. Demonstrate capability and it responds. Light a fire, get water. Solve a puzzle, get food. Stagnate, get nothing. The Settlers found workarounds — exploits, side paths, places to access supplies without progressing.

## Challenge Subjects

The Torus tests everything — physical, practical, intellectual. Core areas include:

- **Algebra**: Substitution, equivalence, unknowns. Made physical — balance mechanisms, weight relationships, proportional systems
- **Geometry**: Spatial reasoning, angles, symmetry, shapes fitting together. The Torus is itself a geometric structure — navigation is geometry
- **Logic and reasoning**: Deduction, elimination, if/then. Doors and mechanisms that require correct inference
- **Memory techniques**: The Torus rewards remembering. Patterns from earlier chambers recur. Sequences that must be held in mind. Spatial memory for navigation. The litanies themselves are things worth memorising
- **Probability**: Judgment under uncertainty. Incomplete information. Which door, which path, when the odds favour risk. Later: expected value, Bayesian updating — made physical through situations where you must decide with imperfect knowledge
- **Physics and engineering**: How things move, fall, flow. Mechanisms to build or redirect. Later: pressure, heat, force
- **Science and observation**: Watching how the environment behaves before acting. Hypothesis, test, conclusion — learned by doing, never named
- **Error detection**: Spotting when something is wrong. Heuristics, fallacies, traps that look right but aren't. The Torus is full of things designed to catch you if you don't check your work — or your assumptions
- **Estimation and Fermi problems**: How far, how much, how long, how many. The Torus rarely gives exact numbers. Rough answers fast, from what you can see. Being roughly right keeps you alive
- **Decision making under uncertainty**: Two doors, no information, consequences are permanent. When to gather more information and when to act. Reversible vs irreversible. The cost of waiting vs the cost of being wrong
- **Systems thinking and mental models**: How parts connect. Cause chains, feedback loops, bottlenecks, leverage points, emergence. The Torus is a system — seeing only the thing in front of you kills you

Starting level: what a bright nine-year-old can reach. Growing with the protagonist as she ages and the story progresses.
